Cardiovascular System

Disorders of Heart Rate & Rhythm

🧬 Aetiology
  • Sinus bradycardia: vagal tone ↑, hypothyroidism, athletes, inferior MI, drugs
  • AV block 1st: prolonged PR (>200ms); benign
  • AV block 2nd Mobitz I (Wenckebach): progressive PR lengthening → dropped beat; usually inferior MI
  • AV block 2nd Mobitz II: sudden dropped beat; pre-syncope, progresses to complete block
  • Complete heart block (3rd): atria and ventricles beat independently; inferior or anterior MI, Lyme disease, post-cardiac surgery
  • Sick sinus syndrome: sinus node dysfunction; elderly
  • Drugs: β-blockers, non-DHP CCBs (diltiazem, verapamil), digoxin, amiodarone
💬 Symptoms & Signs
  • Syncope / presyncope / dizziness
  • Fatigue, exercise intolerance
  • Dyspnoea
  • Cannon A waves (CHB) — atria contracting against closed TV
  • HR <60 bpm; may be regular or irregular
  • Hypotension if haemodynamically compromised
🔬 Investigations
  • ECG: key; determine block type
  • 24hr Holter monitor (intermittent symptoms)
  • TFTs, electrolytes (K⁺, Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺)
  • Troponin/cardiac enzymes if MI suspected
  • Echo if structural disease suspected
💊 Management
  • Acute/unstable: Atropine IV (0.5–1 mg); transcutaneous pacing
  • Mobitz II / CHB / symptomatic: Permanent pacemaker (PPM)
  • Stop offending drugs if iatrogenic
  • Treat underlying cause (e.g., thyroxine for hypothyroidism)
  • 1st degree & Wenckebach: usually no treatment needed
⚡ High Yield
  • Inferior MI → AV block (RCA supplies AV node in 90%)
  • Anterior MI → complete block due to infranodal disease → poor prognosis
  • Mobitz II always needs pacing; Wenckebach usually does not
  • Cannon A waves = complete heart block hallmark
CDRIA Point Jon Lee — ECG Approach to Heart Blocks & Bradycardia
  • ECG recognition: 1st degree = PR >200ms (5 little squares). Marked if >300ms. Usually a normal variant or drugs — not clinically significant alone
  • 2nd degree Mobitz I (Wenckebach): PR progressively lengthens → dropped beat. Causes: beta-blockers, CCBs, athletes, increased vagal tone, inferior AMI. Usually benign — AV node suppression is often reversible. Low risk of progressing to complete block. Manage with atropine if symptomatic; rarely needs pacemaker
  • 2nd degree Mobitz II: Normal PR → normal PR → sudden dropped beat, no PR lengthening. Causes: anterior AMI, cardiac surgery, infiltrative disease (amyloidosis), autoimmune (SLE), beta-blockers, CCBs, hyperkalaemia. Level of block is below AV node (His-Purkinje) — structural damage. Risk of asystole ~35%/year. ADMIT. Monitor. Pacemaker
  • 3rd degree (Complete Heart Block): No relationship between P waves and QRS. Ventricular rate 30–40 bpm. Presents with severe bradycardia, dizziness worse on exertion, or collapse. Causes: inferior AMI, beta-blockers, CCBs, digoxin. HIGH risk of asystole and sudden cardiac death. ADMIT! MONITOR. Pacemaker
  • Clinical rule: "1st and Mobitz I = not so bad; Mobitz II and 3rd degree = BAD." Any symptomatic block warrants admission and monitoring
  • Palpitations differential for slow/skipped beats: Most likely — sinus bradycardia ± ventricular ectopic; Serious — CHB or Mobitz II; Atypical — vagal response, drug-induced, hypothyroidism, hypothermia, elite athlete

🧬 AF Risk Factors
  • Hypertension (most common)
  • Ischaemic heart disease
  • Mitral valve disease
  • Heart failure
  • Thyrotoxicosis, alcohol ("holiday heart")
  • Post-cardiac surgery, pneumonia, PE
💊 AF Management
  • Rate control: β-blocker, diltiazem, digoxin (if HF)
  • Rhythm control: DC cardioversion, flecainide (if no structural disease), amiodarone
  • Anticoagulation: CHA₂DS₂-VASc score → DOAC/warfarin (score ≥1 in men, ≥2 in women)
  • Pulmonary vein isolation: catheter ablation for paroxysmal AF
  • Treat underlying cause
⚡ High Yield
  • AF → LA dilation → mural thrombus → embolic stroke (esp. middle cerebral artery)
  • Adenosine terminates SVT but NOT AF/flutter
  • WPW + AF = AVOID AV nodal blockers (digoxin, verapamil, adenosine) which can lead to VF; use procainamide or DC cardioversion
  • CHA₂DS₂-VASc: CHF/HTN/Age≥75(×2)/DM/Stroke(×2)/Vascular disease/Age 65–74/Sex female
CDRIA Point Jon Lee — Clinical Approach to Tachyarrhythmias
  • History framework — think Predisposing / Precipitating / Complicating:
  • Risk factors: Ever had this before? Known AF or SVT? On medications for it? How did they stop it last time?
  • Precipitating: Infection screen (fever, focal sx); medication compliance; ETOH; caffeine; illicit drugs; hyperthyroid symptoms; PE risk screen
  • Complicating: SOB, chest pain (already covered); focal neuro symptoms for stroke — but only ask if it sounds like AF; ischaemic events if tachycardia with underlying IHD
  • ECG approach (in order): Is it fast? → Narrow or Broad? → Regular or Irregular? → P waves present? — Top 3 narrow complex tachycardia: Sinus tach / SVT / Rapid AF. "Broad = more concerning; Narrow = less concerning"
  • Rapid AF (RAF) 6-step management: [1] Rate control + Ix/Rx reversible causes [2] Most self-revert within 12–24hrs [3] DCR if unstable/not responding [4] CHADSVASC for anticoagulation [5] Admit if ongoing reversible causes [6] Consult Cardiology if unsure
  • SVT management: Valsalva / carotid sinus massage first → if fails: Adenosine (large bore IV in cubital fossa, defib pads on, warn patient — they will feel awful briefly)
  • Type II AMI: Troponin rise caused by a non-ischaemic primary issue (e.g. prolonged SVT causing rate-related strain, sepsis, severe HF) — not a true plaque rupture event; always discuss with Cardiology if uncertain

🧬 Types & Causes
  • VT: ≥3 consecutive ventricular beats at >100 bpm; wide QRS (>120ms)
  • VF: chaotic; no coordinated contraction; cardiac arrest
  • Torsades de Pointes: polymorphic VT on long QT (drugs, electrolytes, congenital)
  • Causes: IHD/MI (most common), cardiomyopathy, electrolyte (↓K⁺, ↓Mg²⁺), long QT syndrome, Brugada syndrome, cocaine
  • Sudden cardiac death: usually fatal VF; 90% have severe coronary atherosclerosis
💊 Management
  • Pulseless VT/VF: Defibrillation + CPR immediately (ALS protocol)
  • Stable VT: Amiodarone IV; consider synchronized DC cardioversion
  • Unstable VT: Synchronized DC cardioversion
  • Torsades: IV MgSO₄; correct electrolytes; stop QT-prolonging drugs
  • Long-term: ICD (implantable cardioverter-defibrillator) for high-risk patients
  • Amiodarone — chronic suppression
  • Treat underlying cause
⚡ High Yield
  • Most common early complication of MI = arrhythmia (within first 24hrs)
  • VF is the most common cause of sudden cardiac death
  • Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy = most common cause of sudden cardiac death in young athletes
  • QT-prolonging drugs: quinidine, sotalol, amiodarone, haloperidol, azithromycin, chloroquine
CDRIA Point Jon Lee — Broad Complex Tachycardia, Brugada & Torsades
  • Golden rule on ECG: "Broad = Bad? Narrow = Not so bad?" — any broad complex tachycardia must be treated as VT until proven otherwise. Most common tachyarrhythmia is sinus tachycardia; conscious VT = broad, regular, patient still conscious
  • VF is an arrest state — patients don't present with palpitations; they present with cardiac arrest. A conscious patient chatting to you is not in VF
  • Torsades de Pointes (Long QT): QTc >440ms (men) / >460ms (women) = prolonged QT. QTc >500ms = significant risk of torsades. Causes: congenital, hypokalaemia, hypocalcaemia, drug overdoses (see QT-prolonging drugs). ECG: polymorphic VT twisting around isoelectric line. Treatment: IV MgSO₄; defibrillation if unstable; ICD long term. Correct electrolytes; stop culprit drugs
  • Brugada Syndrome: Sodium channel mutation; structurally normal heart. High risk of sudden cardiac death via VF/VT. Diagnosis requires: [1] "Coved" ST elevation in V1–V3 AND [2] ≥1 of: documented VF/polymorphic VT, FHx sudden cardiac death <45, syncope, nocturnal agonal respiration. Management: ICD
  • Ventricular ectopics: Single premature wide complex with no preceding P wave; usually isolated and benign; can mimic ST elevation — look for consistency and absence of P wave to distinguish

Coronary Heart Disease

🧬 Pathophysiology
  • Atherosclerotic plaque disruption (rupture or erosion) → platelet aggregation → thrombus formation
  • Unstable angina (UA): partial occlusion → no myocardial necrosis ; ST depression or T wave changes
  • NSTEMI: partial occlusion → subendocardial necrosis; ST depression or T wave changes
  • STEMI: usually persistent, near‑complete or complete occlusion of an epicardial artery → transmural infarction; ST elevation
  • LAD (45%): anterior wall + anterior septum
  • RCA (30%): posterior wall, posterior septum, papillary muscles
  • LCx (25%): lateral wall
🩺 Symptoms & Signs
  • Severe, crushing, central chest pain >20 min; may be pressure/heaviness rather than sharp pain
  • Radiation to left arm, jaw, neck, or back
  • Diaphoresis, nausea, vomiting; sense of impending doom
  • Dyspnoea, pallor, cold clammy skin
  • Not reliably relieved by GTN in ACS (unlike stable exertional angina)
  • Silent MI/atypical presentations common in diabetics, elderly, and some women (e.g. dyspnoea, fatigue, epigastric discomfort)
  • Signs: tachycardia, hypotension (cardiogenic shock), S4, S3 in LV failure, pulmonary crackles
🧪 Investigations & Biomarkers
  • Troponin I: gold standard
    • Detectable rise as early as 1–3 h after symptom onset; use serial measurements
    • MI (NSTEMI/STEMI) = troponin >99th percentile with a rise and/or fall plus evidence of ischaemia (symptoms, ECG, imaging)
    • Unstable angina: ischaemic symptoms ± ECG changes, but Troponin I remains within reference range on serial testing
  • CK‑MB: may help in suspected reinfarction (falls back to normal sooner than troponin)
  • ECG:
    • STEMI: new ST elevation in a coronary territory (or new LBBB considered STEMI‑equivalent)
    • UA/NSTEMI: ST depression and/or T‑wave inversion, or normal ECG; changes may be dynamic (serial ECGs).[web:8]
  • CXR: pulmonary oedema
  • Echo: wall motion abnormalities
  • Coronary angiography: definitive for STEMI
💊 Management
  • Immediate (all suspected ACS):
    • MONITOR: cardiac monitor, serial ECGs, IV access, bloods including Troponin I
    • Dual antiplatelet: Aspirin 300 mg STAT + P2Y12 inhibitor (ticagrelor preferred over clopidogrel unless high bleeding risk)
    • Anticoagulation: LMWH (e.g. enoxaparin) or fondaparinux, or UFH
    • Oxygen only if SpO₂ < 90–94% (avoid routine high‑flow O₂ in normoxic MI)
    • Sublingual/IV GTN for chest pain and hypertension if haemodynamically stable
    • Avoid GTN in SBP < 90 mmHg, marked brady/tachycardia, suspected RV infarction (inferior MI with RV involvement), or recent PDE‑5 inhibitor use
    • Morphine for persistent severe pain/anxiety (cautious use; may delay oral P2Y12 absorption)
  • STEMI:
    • Immediate reperfusion: Primary PCI with door‑to‑balloon ≤90 min (or as per local target)
    • Fibrinolysis if timely PCI unavailable and no contraindications, ideally within 12 h of symptom onset
  • UA/NSTEMI:
    • Early invasive strategy: Early PCI for high-risk; risk stratify (e.g. GRACE >140, dynamic ST/T changes, ongoing pain, haemodynamic or electrical instability)
  • Secondary prevention: high‑intensity statin, ACEi/ARB, β‑blocker, long‑term single antiplatelet (usually aspirin), cardiac rehabilitation, aggressive risk‑factor modification

⚡ High Yield
  • Troponin I = most sensitive AND specific for MI (gold standard)
  • UA vs NSTEMI: same symptoms and ECG; the formal difference is troponin‑defined necrosis :
    • Normal serial Troponin → UA
    • Elevated Troponin with rise/fall → NSTEMI
  • LAD = "widow maker" — most commonly involved vessel in MI
  • In suspected RV infarction, prioritize IV fluids, avoid nitrates/diuretics/morphine/ACEi until haemodynamics are stable
  • Reperfusion injury: contraction band necrosis; calcium influx + free radicals
  • Rupture most likely 4–7 days post-MI (macrophage digestion = weakest point)
  • Dressler syndrome: autoimmune fibrinous pericarditis 1–8 weeks post-MI; treat with NSAIDs + colchicine; avoid anticoagulation
CDRIA Point Jon Lee — Clinical Approach to Chest Pain / ACS
  • Chest pain history: When did it start? Where is it? Radiation (neck/jaw/arm/back/abdomen)? Constant or intermittent? Severity 0–10? Worse with movement, deep breath, palpation, or exertion? Character (sharp/dull/heavy/pressure/stabbing)?
  • Associated features to screen: Sweating, Vomiting, SOB, Palpitations, Dizziness, Calf pain/swelling, Cough, Fevers
  • Cardiac risk stratification: DM, HTN, hypercholesterolaemia, FHx of AMI/angina/stents/bypasses, smoking. Indian/Sri Lankan ethnicity is an independent risk factor. Biggest RF = having had it before
  • ECG — 1st question: "Is it a STEMI?" STEMI = ST elevation >1 little square (>2 squares in V2/V3), in ≥2 contiguous leads, that is NEW, with active chest pain
  • ECG — 2nd question: "Could it be a NSTEMI?" → ST depression or T inversion in arterial distribution; are changes evolving (dynamic)? Check OLD ECG then do SERIAL ECGs. You CANNOT diagnose NSTEMI on ECG alone — prove it with serial troponins
  • Beware: Diabetic and possibly female silent AMI — may have no classic chest pain
  • Management mnemonic — "I'm Acting General Manager ABCD Cath": IV access; Aspirin + GTN + Morphine (pain relief) → Atorvastatin, Beta-blocker, Clexane (LMWH), DAPT (Clopidogrel or Ticagrelor) → Cath lab
  • STEMI vs NSTEMI — the only real difference is time to Cath: STEMI → <90 min; NSTEMI → within 24hrs
  • Explaining to patients (5 steps): [1] Check their understanding [2] Explain what's happening to the body (normal vs what's happening to them) [3] Tie in each symptom [4] Explain each investigation and medication/procedure and how it helps [5] Check understanding and invite questions

🧬 Pathophysiology
  • Stable angina: >70% stenosis → supply-demand mismatch on exertion; NO necrosis; ST depression on ECG; relieved by rest/GTN
  • Resting perfusion is adequate; no ongoing ischaemia at rest and no myocardial necrosis → troponin remains normal
  • Chronic ischaemic heart disease: Gradual myocardial damage → CHF
🩺 Symptoms & Signs
  • Central or left‑sided chest pressure/heaviness (not sharp), ± radiation to arm, neck, jaw or back
  • Provoked by exertion (walking uphill, stairs) or emotional stress; relieved within minutes by rest and/or sublingual GTN
  • Symptoms are predictable and reproducible with a given level of exertion/emotional stress and resolve with rest/GTN within minutes
🧪 Investigations
  • Baseline: resting ECG, FBC, U&E, lipids, HbA1c, renal function, fasting glucose
  • Functional testing: exercise ECG, stress echo, or nuclear perfusion scan to demonstrate inducible ischaemia
  • Anatomical imaging: CT coronary angiography (CTCA) or invasive coronary angiography for high‑risk or unclear cases
  • Troponin is not elevated in stable angina (if elevated with compatible symptoms → think NSTE‑ACS rather than “stable” angina)
💊 Management
  • Acute: Sublingual GTN (glyceryl trinitrate) spray
  • Long-term symptom control: β-blocker (first-line) OR rate-limiting CCB; long-acting nitrates; nicorandil; ivabradine
  • Anti-platelet: Aspirin 75mg daily; statin
  • ACE inhibitor: if DM or LV dysfunction
  • Revascularisation: PCI or CABG if refractory or multi-vessel disease
  • Risk factor modification (smoking, hypertension, DM, dyslipidaemia)
CDRIA Point Jon Lee — IHD/Angina Pattern Recognition & Wellen's Syndrome
  • Classic IHD/angina pattern (Red Flags): Central or left-sided chest pressure/heaviness radiating to arm, neck, jaw or back; establish ONSET and DURATION; NOT worse with deep inspiration or palpation; worse with exertion; associated N/V, SOB, dizziness. Risk factor profile: personal hx IHD/HTN/cholesterol/DM, FHx, smoking. Note: Indian/Sri Lankan ethnicity is an independent risk factor for IHD
  • ECG approach: ST depression = subendocardial ischaemia (stable or unstable angina). Compare with old ECG — is this NEW? Serial ECGs to look for dynamic change
  • Wellen's Syndrome (must not miss): Requires [1] biphasic or deeply inverted T waves in V2/V3 on ECG AND [2] history of recent chest pain that has since resolved. Highly specific for a critical stenosis of the LAD. ADMIT for urgent angiogram. Do NOT perform a stress test — risk of precipitating MI
  • Treatment rule: Use GTN as a diagnostic/therapeutic tool — significant relief of chest pain after sublingual GTN supports an ischaemic cause. If no relief, broaden your differential

🧬 Aetiology & RF
  • Atherosclerosis of peripheral arteries (aorto-iliac, femoro-popliteal most common)
  • Risk factors: smoking (strongest), DM, hypertension, hyperlipidaemia, age, male sex
  • Often coexists with CAD and cerebrovascular disease
💬 Clinical Features — Fontaine Classification
  • Stage I: Asymptomatic; ABPI <0.9
  • Stage II: Intermittent claudication — calf pain on walking, relieved by rest
  • Stage III: Rest pain — forefoot pain at night, relieved hanging leg down
  • Stage IV: Tissue loss — ulcers, gangrene (critical limb ischaemia)
  • Signs: absent pulses, pallor, cool, dependent rubor, hair loss, shiny skin, slow cap refill
🔬 Investigations
  • ABPI (Ankle-Brachial Pressure Index): <0.9 = PAD; <0.5 = critical ischaemia
  • Duplex USS: assess stenosis
  • CT/MR angiography
  • Formal angiography pre-intervention
💊 Management
  • Medical: Smoking cessation, antiplatelet (aspirin/clopidogrel), statin, BP control, DM management
  • Exercise: Supervised exercise program (claudication) — first-line
  • Cilostazol: phosphodiesterase inhibitor for claudication
  • Revascularisation: Angioplasty ± stenting or bypass surgery (rest pain/critical ischaemia)
  • Amputation: last resort for non-viable limb
🧬 Aortic Aneurysm
  • AAA (Abdominal): >3 cm; infrarenal most common; atherosclerosis, smoking, hypertension; M:F = 6:1; screening USS at 65yrs in men
  • Thoracic: hypertension, cystic medial necrosis (Marfan, EDS), syphilis ("tree-bark" aorta)
  • Sx: usually asymptomatic; pulsatile abdominal mass; back/abdominal pain
  • Ruptured AAA: triad: hypotension + pulsatile mass + back pain; surgical emergency; 80% mortality
  • Repair: elective at ≥5.5 cm (EVAR or open); emergency open repair if ruptured
🔬 Aortic Dissection
  • Intimal tear → blood dissects through media
  • Stanford A: involves ascending aorta; surgical emergency; mortality 1–2%/hour if untreated
  • Stanford B: descending aorta only; medical management (unless complications)
  • Risk factors: hypertension (most common), Marfan syndrome, bicuspid aortic valve, cocaine, trauma
  • Symptoms: sudden tearing/ripping chest pain radiating to back; pulse deficit; BP difference between arms >20mmHg; aortic regurgitation murmur
  • Ix: CT aortogram (gold standard); CXR: widened mediastinum
  • Mx: Type A → urgent surgery; Type B → IV labetalol (target SBP 100–120)
⚡ High Yield
  • Marfan syndrome → cystic medial necrosis → aortic root dilation → type A dissection + aortic regurgitation
  • Syphilis → obliterative endarteritis of vasa vasorum → medial necrosis → "tree-bark" thoracic aorta
  • AAA most common complication = rupture; other = thromboembolism, ureteric obstruction
CDRIA Point Jon Lee — Aortic Dissection: Clinical Pearls
  • Incidence: Rare — approximately 3 per 100,000 per year
  • Mortality without treatment: Type A → ~50% die within 3 days; Type B → ~10% die within a month
  • Clinical red flags to trigger suspicion: Tearing/ripping severe chest pain radiating through to the back, or to neck/head/shoulders; BP difference between arms (>20 mmHg) or radio-radial delay; hypertensive patient; ± focal neurology (stroke from carotid involvement)
  • Key rule: If you think it, you must rule it out — CT Thoracic Angiogram is the investigation of choice
  • Management summary: Type A → urgent surgery; Type B → BP control (IV labetalol, target SBP 100–120 mmHg)

🧬 Classification & Causes
  • Primary (essential) 95%: genetic + environmental; no identifiable cause
  • Secondary 5%: renal artery stenosis, CKD, primary hyperaldosteronism (Conn), phaeochromocytoma, Cushing, coarctation, drugs (COCP, NSAIDs)
  • Grade 1: BP 140–159/90–99
  • Grade 2: BP 160–179/100–109
  • Grade 3/Severe: ≥180/110
  • Hypertensive emergency: severely elevated BP + end-organ damage (hypertensive encephalopathy, AKI, aortic dissection, acute LVF)
⚠️ Target Organ Damage
  • Heart: LVH, IHD, heart failure, AF
  • Brain: stroke, hypertensive encephalopathy
  • Kidney: arteriolonephrosclerosis → CKD; malignant HTN → fibrinoid necrosis → "flea-bitten" kidney
  • Eye: hypertensive retinopathy (AV nipping, haemorrhages, papilloedema)
  • Aorta: atherosclerosis, dissection, aneurysm
💊 Management (Heart Foundation Australia)
  • Lifestyle (all): ↓salt, ↓alcohol, weight loss, exercise, healthy diet, smoking cessation
  • First-line: ACEi/ARB, CCB, or thiazide-like diuretic — do NOT combine ACEi + ARB
  • CKD + albuminuria: Prefer ACEi or ARB
  • Step 2: ACEi/ARB + CCB or thiazide-like diuretic
  • Step 3: ACEi/ARB + CCB + thiazide-like diuretic
  • Resistant HTN: Add spironolactone; check adherence and secondary causes
  • Beta-blockers: Not first-line; use only if CAD, HF, or AF
  • Target: <140/90 mmHg; consider ≤120 mmHg systolic in high-risk (shared decision-making)
  • Hypertensive emergency: IV labetalol; lower BP gradually
🔬 Investigations
  • ABPM (ambulatory BP monitoring): confirm diagnosis
  • Urine dipstick (protein/blood), serum creatinine, eGFR
  • Fasting glucose, lipids
  • ECG: LVH (S in V1 + R in V5 >35mm)
  • Echo if symptomatic
  • Renal USS, aldosterone:renin ratio, urinary catecholamines if secondary suspected

CDRIA Point Jon Lee — Aortic Stenosis & LBBB on ECG
  • AS clinical pattern to recognise: Typical exertional chest pain with SOB and/or dizziness — these symptoms are progressive. Key red flag: this is a valvular cause of chest pain; listen for the ejection systolic murmur at the right upper sternal border radiating to the carotids on examination
  • LBBB and AS: LBBB is a known cause of new LBBB on ECG. A new LBBB is almost always pathological — check OLD ECGs. If LBBB is new in the context of chest pain, treat as STEMI-equivalent until proven otherwise (Sgarbossa's criteria apply)
  • ECG in LBBB — Appropriate Discordance: In leads with a negative QRS, ST segments appear elevated (normal for BBB, NOT a STEMI). In leads with positive QRS, ST segments appear depressed. This is normal for the block and can mimic ischaemia. Use Sgarbossa's criteria to identify true STEMI in the context of LBBB
  • Key LBBB rule: [1] Wide QRS → look at V1 and V6 (LBBB = 'W' in V1 + 'M' in V6; RBBB = 'M' in V1 + 'W'/slurred S in V6). [2] A NEW LBBB = pathological until proven otherwise. [3] Cannot diagnose STEMI by normal rules if BBB present — apply Sgarbossa's criteria
  • Murmur bedside approach: Listen at the four valve areas and ask the patient to hold their breath for 4–5 beats when needed. The area where the murmur is loudest usually points to the culprit valve. If you hear a murmur, palpate the pulse: sound with the pulse = systolic. For systolic murmurs, listen to the carotids for aortic stenosis and the axilla for mitral regurgitation.

🧬 Left Heart Failure
  • Causes: IHD/MI (most common), hypertension, dilated cardiomyopathy, valvular disease (MR, AS, AR), myocarditis
  • Symptoms: dyspnoea, PND, orthopnoea
  • Signs: bibasal crackles, S3 gallop, tachycardia, displaced apex beat
  • Why orthopnoea? Lying flat redistributes venous blood to thorax → ↑ pulmonary venous pressure
  • Why PND? Peripheral oedema reabsorbed overnight → mobilises into circulation → acute pulmonary oedema at 2–3 AM
  • S3 gallop: rapid ventricular filling into a dilated, low-compliance ventricle; specific for volume overload/systolic HF
  • Heart failure cells: haemosiderin-laden macrophages in lungs; result of repeated alveolar microhaemorrhages in chronic pulmonary oedema
  • Reduced renal perfusion → RAAS → fluid retention → worsens HF (vicious cycle)
🧬 Right Heart Failure
  • Causes: Left HF (most common); cor pulmonale (COPD, OSA, IPF); congenital HD; tricuspid/pulmonary valve disease; PE
  • Symptoms: peripheral oedema, abdominal bloating, anorexia
  • Signs: raised JVP, peripheral pitting oedema, hepatomegaly, ascites, pleural effusion
  • Raised JVP: reflects ↑ right atrial pressure; measure at 45° (vertical height above sternal angle)
  • Nutmeg liver: hepatic venous congestion → centrilobular necrosis; gross appearance resembles nutmeg cross-section
  • Cor pulmonale: RV failure caused by lung disease/pulmonary HTN — not by left-sided failure
🔬 Investigations
  • BNP/NT-proBNP: elevated; confirms HF; guides therapy titration
  • Echo (most important): EF <40% = HFrEF; EF ≥50% = HFpEF; assesses valves, wall motion
  • CXR findings:
    • Cardiomegaly → cardiothoracic ratio >0.5 on PA
    • Upper lobe diversion → early; pulmonary venous pressure >12 mmHg
    • Kerley B lines → interstitial oedema; short horizontal lines at lung bases
    • Bat-wing oedema → alveolar oedema; perihilar haziness = severe/acute
  • ECG: LBBB (→ consider CRT), AF (common precipitant), LVH, ischaemia
  • Bloods: FBC, U&E, TFTs (hypo/hyperthyroid can precipitate HF), LFTs, iron studies (iron deficiency very common — treatable), troponin (rule out ACS as precipitant)
💊 Management — Four Pillars (HFrEF)
  • All four have Class I mortality benefit evidence (ESC 2021 / AHA-ACC 2022)
  • ACEi / ARNI (sacubitril/valsartan):
    • ACEi blocks RAAS → ↓ Ang II and aldosterone
    • ARNI = ARB + neprilysin inhibitor → blocks RAAS and preserves BNP/ANP
    • ARNI is superior to ACEi alone
    • ⚠️ Never combine ACEi + ARNI → angioedema risk
  • β-blocker (bisoprolol, carvedilol, metoprolol succinate):
    • Blocks SNS over-activation
    • Slows HR → more diastolic filling time
    • Reverses cardiac remodelling over months
    • ⚠️ Start low, go slow — do NOT start in acute decompensation
  • MRA — spironolactone / eplerenone:
    • Blocks aldosterone → ↓ Na⁺ retention and myocardial fibrosis
    • ⚠️ Monitor K⁺ — hyperkalaemia risk (especially with ACEi)
    • Eplerenone preferred in males (spironolactone → gynaecomastia)
  • SGLT2i — empagliflozin / dapagliflozin:
    • Originally a diabetes drug — found to dramatically ↓ HF hospitalisations
    • Mechanisms: osmotic diuresis, ↓ preload, anti-fibrotic, mitochondrial effects
    • Now recommended across HFrEF and HFpEF
  • Furosemide (diuretic): symptom relief only — no mortality benefit; used for acute decongestion
  • HFpEF: no drug proven to reduce mortality; treat underlying cause; diuretics for symptoms; SGLT2i for symptom benefit

CDRIA Point Jon Lee — CCF Exacerbation & Acute Pulmonary Oedema
  • Classify the syndrome: HFrEF = LVEF ≤40%; HFpEF = LVEF ≥50%. The common underlying causes are IHD first, then hypertension, valvular disease (AS/MS), congenital disease such as HCM, infiltrative disease such as amyloid, and inflammatory causes such as myocarditis.
  • Common exacerbation triggers: missed diuretics, missed fluid restriction, infection, and new ischaemic events. Ask baseline vs now for exercise tolerance, orthopnoea, PND, swelling, and usual oxygen requirement.
  • Useful investigations: CXR, ECG, BNP, troponin, and UECr help screen and guide management; echocardiogram is the investigation of choice. BNP is released by stretched cardiac myocytes in fluid overload and is useful for heart failure and other overload states.
  • APO: Hypoxic, Tachypneic and Hypertensive
  • Immediate APO management: IV access, sit upright, oxygen, GTN, IV furosemide, and CPAP/BiPAP when needed.
  • Long-term HFrEF treatment focus: SGLT2 inhibitor, cardioselective β-blocker, ACEi/ARB/ARNI, plus diuretics for symptomatic congestion.

CDRIA Point Jon Lee — HCM: Clinical Pattern & ECG
  • HCM clinical presentation pattern: Exertional chest pain, SOB, dizziness, palpitations, or collapse in a young person. Prevalence 1 in 500; annual mortality 1–2%. Number 1 cause of sudden cardiac death in young people
  • Genetics: ~60% is a genetic disorder, primarily autosomal dominant. Causes issues through decreased cardiac output (LVOTO or poor filling), mitral regurgitation, or arrhythmias
  • ECG findings: LVH (tall R waves in chest/limb leads) + narrow Q waves inferiorly or laterally. If you see this pattern in a young patient with exertional collapse → think HCM
  • Definitive diagnosis: Echocardiogram for LV wall thickness (septum >15mm or ASH)
  • Management: Beta-blockers or Ca channel blockers (symptom control); ICD for high-risk patients (hx of VT/VF, FHx sudden death, massive hypertrophy, syncope); septal myectomy or alcohol septal ablation if LVOTO refractory to medication
  • Key clinical exam rule: Mid-systolic ejection murmur at LSE — gets louder with Valsalva/standing and softer with squatting (dynamic obstruction)

🧬 Pericarditis — Causes
  • Viral (most common): Coxsackie B, Echovirus, EBV, influenza; "idiopathic" usually viral
  • Post-MI: fibrinous pericarditis (1–3 days); Dressler syndrome (autoimmune, 2–8 weeks)
  • Uraemia (fibrinous → "bread and butter")
  • Autoimmune: SLE, rheumatoid arthritis
  • Bacterial (purulent), TB (haemorrhagic), malignancy
💬 Pericarditis — Features
  • Sharp pleuritic chest pain; worse lying flat, leaning forward relieves
  • Pericardial friction rub (pathognomonic)
  • Fever, malaise
  • ECG: Saddle-shaped (concave up) ST elevation in multiple leads (not territory-specific); PR depression
  • Troponin may be mildly elevated (myopericarditis)
  • Mx: NSAIDs (ibuprofen/aspirin) + colchicine (reduces recurrence); avoid anticoagulants if possible (risk of haemopericardium); steroids if autoimmune/refractory; rest from exercise for 3 months
🔬 Cardiac Tamponade
  • Fluid accumulation in pericardial space → ↑intrapericardial pressure → compression of heart
  • As little as 200mL if rapid; up to 2L if slow accumulation
  • Beck's Triad: JVP elevated, hypotension, muffled heart sounds
  • Pulsus paradoxus: >10 mmHg fall in SBP during inspiration
  • ECG: low voltage + electrical alternans
  • Echo: RA/RV diastolic collapse
  • Mx: Pericardiocentesis (needle aspiration) — emergency
⚠️ Constrictive Pericarditis
  • TB or pyogenic → fibrous thickening + calcification of pericardium
  • Prevents cardiac filling → mimics right HF
  • Kussmaul sign (JVP rises on inspiration)
  • CXR: pericardial calcification
  • Mx: Pericardiectomy (definitive)
CDRIA Point Jon Lee — Pericardial Tamponade: Red Flags
  • Pattern to recognise (from chest pain differentials): Pleuritic chest pain; pain worse in certain positions (sitting forward relieves); tachycardia; SOBOE; hypotension
  • Risk factor screen: Concurrent viral-type illness; pregnancy; known malignancy; autoimmune conditions (SLE, RA, scleroderma)
  • Key clinical rule: Any patient with pleuritic chest pain + haemodynamic instability (tachycardia + hypotension) + muffled heart sounds → suspect tamponade and act immediately

Respiratory System

Obstructive Pulmonary Diseases

🔬 Pathophysiology
  • Reversible airway bronchoconstriction; chronic airway inflammation
  • Atopic (extrinsic): Type I hypersensitivity (IgE-mediated); TH2 response; IL-4 (→IgE), IL-5 (→eosinophils), IL-13
  • Early phase: mast cell degranulation → histamine, leukotrienes C4/D4/E4 → bronchoconstriction
  • Late phase: eosinophilic infiltration → major basic protein → epithelial damage
  • Non-atopic: exercise, aspirin (COX-1 → ↑leukotrienes), cold air, viral infections, occupational
  • Histology: SM hypertrophy, goblet cell hyperplasia, BM thickening, eosinophils, Curschmann spirals, Charcot-Leyden crystals
💬 Symptoms & Signs
  • Episodic dyspnoea, wheeze, cough (worse at night/early morning), chest tightness
  • Triggers: allergens, exercise, viral URTI, cold air, smoke
  • Signs: expiratory wheeze, prolonged expiration, hyperinflation
  • Severe attack: SpO₂ <92%, tachycardia >110, RR >25, PEFR 33–50% predicted
  • Life-threatening: silent chest, cyanosis, bradycardia, exhaustion, PEFR <33%
  • Status asthmaticus: severe, prolonged attack unresponsive to bronchodilators
🔬 Investigations
  • Spirometry: ↓FEV1, ↓FVC, ↓FEV1/FVC (<0.7) — obstructive; >12% and >200mL improvement after bronchodilator = reversibility
  • PEFR: diurnal variation >20% = asthma
  • FeNO (fractional exhaled nitric oxide): elevated in eosinophilic asthma
  • CXR: hyperinflation; rule out other causes
  • Skin prick tests / specific IgE (RAST) if atopic
  • ABG: acute severe — initially ↑pH, ↓pCO₂; normalising CO₂ in attack = DANGER (tiring)
💊 Management (BTS/SIGN Stepwise)
  • Step 1: SABA PRN (salbutamol) — use as reliever at all steps
  • Step 2: Add low-dose ICS (beclometasone 200–400 mcg/day)
  • Step 3: Add LABA to ICS (e.g. salmeterol or formoterol); if no response to LABA → stop LABA, increase ICS to medium dose; consider LTRA (montelukast)
  • Step 4: Increase to medium/high-dose ICS + LABA; consider adding LTRA, theophylline, or LAMA (tiotropium)
  • Step 5: Refer to specialist; consider biologics: anti-IgE (omalizumab), anti-IL-5 (mepolizumab/benralizumab), anti-IL-4/13 (dupilumab); or daily oral prednisolone (last resort)
  • Acute severe: Back-to-back salbutamol nebulisers + ipratropium, IV/oral corticosteroids (prednisolone 40–50 mg or IV hydrocortisone), O₂ to SpO₂ 94–98%, IV MgSO₄ 1.2–2 g over 20 min; escalate to ITU if deteriorating
  • Leukotriene receptor antagonist (montelukast): particularly useful for aspirin-sensitive and exercise-induced asthma
⚡ High Yield
  • Aspirin-sensitive asthma: aspirin → ↓PGE2 (anti-inflammatory) + ↑leukotrienes → bronchoconstriction; treat with leukotriene antagonists
  • Charcot-Leyden crystals = eosinophil-derived; Curschmann spirals = shed airway epithelium
  • Normalising CO₂ during asthma attack = impending respiratory failure
  • Exacerbating factors: ABCDE — Aspirin/NSAIDs, Beta-blockers, Cats/allergens, Dust/Dairy, Exercise
CDRIA Point Jon Lee — Asthma/COPD Exacerbation History
  • Baseline vs now is the key history move: ask current Ventolin requirement compared with usual, whether they use nebs or puffers, whether they use a spacer, baseline exercise tolerance, and their usual oxygen saturations if known.
  • Severity clues on history: home oxygen, regular oral steroids, previous CPAP/BiPAP, previous intubation, and very low baseline exertional tolerance suggest fragile or end-stage obstructive lung disease.
  • Initial burst therapy: 3 rounds over the first hour: 12 puffs salbutamol and 8 puffs ipratropium every 20 minutes, plus steroids and oxygen titrated to the clinical context.
  • VBG/ABG focus: after pH, look especially at CO2. Rising or normalising CO2 in a tiring asthmatic is dangerous because it can signal impending ventilatory failure.

🔬 Chronic Bronchitis
  • Definition: Productive cough ≥3 consecutive months for ≥2 consecutive years
  • Strongly associated with smoking; also air pollution, occupational dust
  • Histology: hypertrophy of bronchial mucous glands; Reid index >50% (normal <40%)
  • "Blue bloater": cyanosis due to CO₂ retention; ↑PaCO₂, ↓PaO₂; oedema
  • Hypoxic drive (dangerous to give high-flow O₂)
  • Cor pulmonale; secondary polycythaemia
🔬 Emphysema
  • Abnormal permanent enlargement of airspaces with destruction of alveolar walls; ↓elastic recoil
  • Mechanism: ↑elastase (from neutrophils/macrophages recruited by smoking) overwhelms α1-antitrypsin → alveolar destruction
  • Centrilobular: respiratory bronchioles; upper lobes; cigarette smoking
  • Panacinar: entire acinus; α1-antitrypsin (α1AT) deficiency; lower lobes; PiZZ homozygous
  • "Pink puffer": pursed-lip breathing; barrel chest; hyperresonance; reduced BS
🔬 Investigations
  • Spirometry: ↓FEV1, ↓FVC, FEV1/FVC <0.7 (no reversibility >12%); ↑TLC, ↑RV (air trapping)
  • CXR: hyperinflation, flattened diaphragm, increased AP diameter, bullae
  • HRCT: shows type and extent of emphysema; bullae
  • ABG: chronic respiratory acidosis (↑CO₂, ↑HCO₃, ↓pH in late disease)
  • α1AT levels (if young, non-smoker)
  • Echo (assess for cor pulmonale)
💊 Management
  • Most important: Smoking cessation (only intervention that slows progression)
  • Mild (FEV1 ≥80%): SABA PRN (salbutamol)
  • Moderate: LAMA (tiotropium) ± LABA
  • Severe with exacerbations: LAMA + LABA + ICS
  • LTOT: Indicated if resting PaO₂ ≤7.3 kPa, or 7.3–8 kPa with cor pulmonale/polycythaemia/nocturnal hypoxaemia; use ≥15 h/day; reduces mortality
  • Pulmonary rehabilitation: improves exercise tolerance and QoL
  • Annual influenza vaccine + pneumococcal vaccine
  • Roflumilast (PDE4 inhibitor): chronic bronchitis + frequent exacerbations
  • Surgery: bullectomy, LVRS, transplant
  • Acute exacerbation: Controlled O₂ (24–28%), nebulised SABA + SAMA, oral prednisolone, antibiotics if purulent sputum, consider NIV (BiPAP) if pH <7.35
⚡ High Yield
  • α1-antitrypsin deficiency: panacinar emphysema (lower lobes) + hepatic cirrhosis; PiZZ genotype; liver disease due to misfolded protein accumulation in hepatocytes
  • COPD → chronic respiratory acidosis: ↓pH, ↑HCO₃, ↑CO₂ (metabolic compensation)
  • O₂ in COPD: target SpO₂ 88–92% (hypoxic drive; high-flow O₂ can suppress drive → CO₂ narcosis)
  • Caplan syndrome: COPD + RA → large pulmonary nodules
CDRIA Point Jon Lee — COPD Exacerbation & CO2 Retention
  • Asthma/COPD overlap in practice: older patients can blur the textbook distinction; practical management often depends on how well they respond to bronchodilators.
  • Exacerbation history questions: compare baseline vs now for Ventolin use, exercise tolerance, oxygen saturations, home oxygen, oral steroids, CPAP/BiPAP, prior intubation, and ongoing smoking.
  • VBG interpretation: look at pH first, then CO2 and bicarbonate. A CO2 of 60–70 may be normal for chronic COPD, so compare with previous gases when available.
  • Severe baseline clues: home oxygen, regular oral steroids, and low baseline exertional tolerance suggest poorly controlled or end-stage obstructive disease.

  • Klebsiella: alcoholics/malnourished; "currant jelly" sputum; cavitating, upper lobe
  • Mycoplasma: crowded settings (schools, barracks, nursing homes, colleges); cold agglutinins; serious Cx: encephalitis, haemolytic anaemia, renal impairment, SJS/erythema multiforme; Rx: Azithromycin or Doxycycline
  • Chlamydia psittaci: exposure to birds (esp. cleaning cages); Rx: Azithromycin or Doxycycline
  • Q fever (Coxiella burnetii): vets, livestock/dairy farmers, abattoir workers; Rx: Doxycycline
  • PCP (Pneumocystis jirovecii): HIV (CD4 <200); "ground-glass" on CT; non-productive cough; Rx: co-trimoxazole (high dose)
  • Varicella pneumonia: most serious complication of chickenpox in adults; develops 1–6 days after rash onset; tachypnoea, cough, dyspnoea, ± haemoptysis, ± pleuritic pain; Rx: IV Acyclovir 7–10 days + supportive
  • Aspergillus: invasive in severe immunodeficiency (neutropenic); chronic necrotising in mild immunodeficiency/chronic lung disease; aspergilloma (fungal ball in cavity); ABPA in atopy/asthma/CF; Rx: Voriconazole (first-line) or Amphotericin B
💊 Management
  • Scoring systems:
    • CURB-65: Confusion, Urea >7, RR ≥30, BP SBP <90/DBP ≤60, Age ≥65 (1 pt each); 0–1 = outpatient; 2 = consider admission; ≥3 = admit ± ICU
    • PSI/PORT: more complex (age + comorbidities + vitals + labs); accounts for age but misses O₂ sats and immunosuppression; PaO₂ requires invasive ABG
    • SMART-COP: predicts need for IRVS (Intensive Respiratory or Vasopressor Support); incorporates O₂ sats + recognises that abnormal vitals matter more in younger patients; Jon Lee's preferred score — more accurate overall but still has hard cutoffs and misses comorbidities/baseline-vs-now
  • Mild typical CAP: outpatient; oral Amoxicillin or Augmentin Duo Forte (amoxicillin/clavulanate)
  • Mild atypical CAP: outpatient; oral Doxycycline
  • Moderdate typical CAP: ADMIT ; IV Benzylpenicillin + oral Doxycycline
  • Severe typical/atypical Pneumonia: ADMIT ; IV Ceftriaxone + Azithromycin
  • HAP/VAP: broad spectrum with pseudomonal cover (Pip-Tazo, Ceftazidime ± aminoglycoside)
  • O₂ to maintain SpO₂ >94%; IV fluids; physiotherapy
  • Pneumococcal vaccine; influenza vaccine; document vaccination profile
  • Viral pneumonias: uncommon but can deteriorate rapidly — get CXR early if vitals trending; diagnose with NPA Respiratory PCR; Influenza → Oseltamivir (Tamiflu); Varicella → IV Acyclovir; SARS-CoV-2 → cytokine storm typically day 6–12; ↑VTE risk (DVT/PE ~20%, ATE ~2%); long-COVID: persistent fatigue, myalgia, weakness, pulmonary dysfunction post-recovery regardless of initial severity
CDRIA Point Jon Lee — Pneumonia Pattern Recognition
  • The 4-Question Framework:
    1. Could it be pneumonia? — Any of: fever, cough (productive/non ± blood), SOB, confusion, ± chest pain, vomiting, rigors, sweats
    2. What sort? — Typical vs atypical; CAP vs HAP; bacterial vs viral; time course; failure to respond to typical cover; recent travel, sick contacts, occupational/animal exposure, immunosuppression, country of origin
    3. How is the patient handling it? — Degree of SOB ( baseline vs now! ); septic sx (vomiting, rigors, sweats, confusion); septic signs (tachycardia, hypoxia, tachypnoea, hypotension)
    4. How likely to get worse? — Comorbidities, age, immunosuppression, failure of initial Rx, rib fractures/trauma
  • Classic pattern (often pleuritic chest pain in older patients): Pain worse with coughing or deep inspiration; productive cough (note productive vs non-productive); fever; ± haemoptysis; SOB; vomiting/rigors
  • Key differentiator from ACS: Pneumonia chest pain is pleuritic (worse with deep breath and movement) — ACS pain is NOT worse with inspiration or palpation
  • Risk stratify for atypical vs CAP: Known COPD or CCF? (clarify baseline SOB vs current). Immunocompromised? Recent hospitalisation/travel?
  • Atypical pneumonia clues: prolonged time course; dry cough; failure to respond to typical antibiotic therapy; high-risk demographic (young/crowded settings = Mycoplasma; bird/cage exposure = Chlamydia psittaci; aircon mist exposure + diarrhoea + ↓Na = Legionella; vets/farmers = Q fever; weight loss + night sweats + haemoptysis + travel = TB)
  • Scoring system limitations: CURB-65 misses O₂ sats, exertional component, baseline vs now, immunosuppression — a patient can score 0 on arrival and deteriorate rapidly. PSI/PORT and SMART-COP are better but still have hard cutoffs. Always combine scores with clinical judgement
  • Differentials not to miss — chest pain + fever: PE (pleuritic pain + tachycardia/hypoxia + risk factors), empyema (persistent fever despite antibiotics)
  • Investigation approach: CXR; FBE, UECr, CRP, ± LFT; blood culture (if febrile); sputum MCS; NPA Respiratory PCR (viral); atypical serology or atypical bacterial PCR (if suspecting atypical); urinary Legionella antigen; ECG
  • Varicella complications (x4): Pneumonia, Encephalitis/meningitis, Ocular involvement, Auricular involvement — auricular + facial nerve palsy = Ramsay Hunt Syndrome
  • Risk factors: Sick contact; recent travel (esp overseas); recent hospitalisation; exposure to birds, moulds, farms, hay, animals; occupation; country of origin ( Aboriginal/Torres Strait Islander descent ); Hx of diabetes, COPD/asthma, cancer; medications (steroids, chemo, immunosuppressives); smoking; nursing home; vaccination profile

🔬 Primary TB
  • Initial infection with Mycobacterium tuberculosis ; airborne droplet nuclei
  • Ghon focus: caseating granuloma in lower lobes (subpleural)
  • Ghon complex: Ghon focus + hilar lymph node involvement → fibrosis + calcification
  • Usually asymptomatic → positive TST/IGRA
  • May disseminate in immunocompromised: miliary TB (haematogenous spread → millet-seed nodules on CXR)
🔬 Secondary (Reactivation) TB
  • Reactivation of latent TB; triggered by immunosuppression (HIV most important), malnutrition, DM, aging
  • Apex of lung (high O₂ tension + poor lymphatics → favours M. tuberculosis growth)
  • Cavitary lesions with caseous necrosis; highly infectious
  • Symptoms: Fever, night sweats, weight loss, productive cough, haemoptysis
  • Systemic spread: meningitis, Pott's disease (vertebral TB — L1 most common), renal TB (sterile pyuria), cervical lymphadenopathy (scrofula)
🔬 Investigations
  • Latent screening: Mantoux TST (PPD); IGRA (Quantiferon-TB Gold — preferred if BCG vaccinated , as TST gives false positive)
  • Active disease: 3× early morning sputum for AFB smear + culture (6–8 weeks) + NAAT/PCR (rapid, same-day result)
  • CXR: apical cavitation; calcified Ghon complex; miliary pattern
  • Biopsy: caseating granulomas with Langhans giant cells
  • HIV test (mandatory in all TB patients); LFTs baseline (for drug monitoring)
💊 Treatment — RIPE
  • 2-month intensive phase: Rifampicin + Isoniazid + Pyrazinamide + Ethambutol (RIPE)
  • 4-month continuation phase: Rifampicin + Isoniazid (RI)
  • Total 6 months pulmonary TB; 12 months CNS/bone TB
  • Pyridoxine (B6): co-prescribe with Isoniazid (prevents peripheral neuropathy)
  • DOT (directly observed therapy) for adherence
  • Notifiable disease — mandatory public health reporting + contact tracing
  • LTBI treatment for close contacts (Isoniazid ± Rifampicin)
  • MDR-TB: resistant to Rifampicin + Isoniazid; second-line agents (fluoroquinolones, injectable aminoglycosides); 18–24 months; specialist care
  • Side effects: Rifampicin (orange urine, hepatotoxicity, CYP450 inducer ↓ efficacy of liver metabolised drugs), INH (peripheral neuropathy, hepatitis), PZA (hyperuricaemia/gout, hepatitis), EMB (optic neuritis — check visual acuity + colour vision before starting)
⚡ High Yield
  • Caseating granulomas = hallmark of TB (vs non-caseating in sarcoidosis)
  • Secondary TB = APEX; miliary TB = haematogenous spread; Pott's disease = spinal TB (L1 most common)
  • Sterile pyuria = renal TB until proven otherwise
  • Isoniazid → peripheral neuropathy; prevented by pyridoxine (B6)
  • Ethambutol → optic neuritis; check visual acuity + colour vision before starting
  • Rifampicin = strong CYP450 inducer → reduces efficacy of OCP, warfarin, DOACs, antiretrovirals
  • IGRA preferred over TST in BCG-vaccinated individuals (avoids false positives)

Upper Airway / Sleep Disorders

🧬 Risk Factors
  • Obesity (most important — neck circumference >40 cm in men, >35 cm in women)
  • Male sex; age; postmenopausal women
  • Retrognathia, macroglossia (acromegaly, hypothyroidism, Down syndrome)
  • Alcohol, sedatives, smoking
  • Nasal obstruction, enlarged tonsils/adenoids
  • Family history
💬 Clinical Features
  • Loud snoring; witnessed apnoeas (partner reports cessation of breathing)
  • Excessive daytime somnolence (Epworth Sleepiness Scale ≥10)
  • Morning headaches (hypercapnia)
  • Nocturia, dry mouth, impotence, poor concentration/memory
  • Signs: obesity, large neck, retrognathia, crowded oropharynx
⚠️ Complications
  • Hypertension (most common complication; non-dipping pattern)
  • Atrial fibrillation
  • Pulmonary hypertension → cor pulmonale
  • Coronary artery disease, stroke
  • Type 2 diabetes mellitus
  • Road traffic accidents (somnolence)
💊 Investigations & Management
  • Diagnosis: Polysomnography (overnight sleep study); AHI (apnoea-hypopnoea index): mild 5–14, moderate 15–30, severe >30
  • Home sleep test: alternative if uncomplicated
  • Lifestyle: Weight loss (most effective long-term), avoid alcohol/sedatives, sleep position
  • CPAP (continuous positive airway pressure): first-line treatment; prevents airway collapse
  • Mandibular advancement device: mild-moderate OSA
  • Surgery: tonsillectomy, uvulopalatopharyngoplasty (selected cases)
  • Treat hypothyroidism/acromegaly if underlying cause

Diseases of the Pleura

🧬 Types & Causes
  • Primary spontaneous: No underlying lung disease; tall thin young men; rupture of apical bleb; trachea shifts to SIDE of collapse
  • Secondary spontaneous: Underlying lung disease (COPD, asthma, TB, CF, Marfan, EDS, PCP); higher morbidity
  • Tension pneumothorax: Penetrating chest wall injury (one-way valve mechanism); air enters but can't exit; tracheal deviation AWAY from side of injury; LIFE-THREATENING
  • Iatrogenic: CVP line, thoracocentesis, positive pressure ventilation, biopsy
  • Traumatic: rib fractures, penetrating injury
💬 Clinical Features & Management
  • Sudden onset pleuritic chest pain; dyspnoea
  • Signs: ↓air entry, hyperresonance, ↓VF on affected side
  • CXR: Visible lung edge; no lung markings beyond; expiratory film accentuates
  • Tension PTX: Beck-like triad + tracheal shift; hypoxia + hypotension = emergency → immediate needle decompression (2nd ICS, MCL), then chest drain
  • Small (<2cm rim): Observe; discharge with advice
  • Large/symptomatic: Aspiration (14G cannula, 2nd ICS MCL) — first-line; if fails → intercostal drain (ICD)
  • Secondary PTX: Lower threshold for drain; admit all
  • Recurrence: VATS pleurodesis/pleurectomy
CDRIA Point Jon Lee — Pneumothorax Pattern Recognition
  • Classic primary spontaneous PTX profile: "Tall thin young man" — often has been exercising or riding a bike; sudden pleuritic chest pain + dyspnoea; may be a recreational drug user (marijuana, ice, speed)
  • Key history clues: Past history of same (biggest single risk factor); sudden onset; pain is pleuritic (worse on inspiration and movement)
  • Do not miss in chest pain workup: PTX is a life-threatening diagnosis in the chest pain differential — always get a CXR; CXR is sufficient to diagnose in most cases
  • Tension PTX rule: Do NOT wait for CXR if patient is haemodynamically unstable with absent breath sounds + tracheal deviation — immediate needle decompression

🔬 Light's Criteria (Exudate if ≥1 met)
  • Pleural fluid protein / serum protein >0.5
  • Pleural fluid LDH / serum LDH >0.6
  • Pleural fluid LDH >2/3 upper limit of normal serum LDH
  • Transudate causes: Heart failure (most common), cirrhosis (hepatic hydrothorax - usually unilateral), nephrotic syndrome, hypoalbuminaemia, hypothyroidism
  • Exudate causes: Malignancy (most common in adults), parapneumonic, TB, PE, rheumatoid arthritis, SLE, pancreatitis, mesothelioma
💬 Clinical Features
  • Dyspnoea, cough, pleuritic chest pain (if inflammatory)
  • Signs: stony dull to percussion (↑↑ compared to consolidation), absent breath sounds, ↓VF, ↓TVF
  • Tracheal deviation away from massive effusion
  • Mesothelioma: recurrent hemorrhagic effusion, asbestos history
  • CXR: blunting of costophrenic angle (>200mL); meniscus sign; complete opacification (massive)
🔬 Investigations
  • USS: confirms fluid; guides thoracocentesis
  • Pleural tap (thoracocentesis): send for protein, LDH, pH, glucose, cytology, MC&S, AFB
  • pH <7.2 = complicated parapneumonic/empyema → drain urgently
  • Glucose <3.3: empyema, malignancy, RA (very low), TB
  • Bloody: malignancy, PE, trauma, TB
  • Milky/turbid: chylothorax (lymphatics) or pseudochylothorax
  • CT chest: identify malignancy, PE; pleural biopsy if TB/malignancy suspected
💊 Management
  • Treat underlying cause (e.g., diuretics for heart failure)
  • Therapeutic aspiration: if symptomatic (up to 1–1.5L per session)
  • Intercostal drain: malignant, parapneumonic, large symptomatic effusion
  • Malignant: pleurodesis (talc) if recurrent; indwelling pleural catheter
  • Surgical decortication for complex/loculated
CDRIA Point Jon Lee — Pleural Effusion Pattern Recognition
  • Clinical pattern: decreased air entry basally, often inspiratory crepitations, dull percussion, and reduced vocal resonance. The finding can be unilateral or bilateral.
  • CXR pattern: blunted costodiaphragmatic recess, solid white opacity rather than patchy white opacity, and possible meniscus. Large effusions can have no lung markings through the fluid.
  • Always ask for the cause: pleural effusion is a diagnosis with an underlying driver. Top causes: infection, CCF, and malignancy; PE can be unilateral or bilateral.
  • Tap the fluid when appropriate: thoracocentesis can be both therapeutic and diagnostic; send MCS, cell count, Gram stain, and cytology.

Interstitial Pulmonary Disease

🧬 Aetiology
  • Most common causes:
  • Cystic fibrosis (most common in young)
  • Post-infective: TB, measles, whooping cough, bacterial pneumonia
  • Immunodeficiency (hypogammaglobulinaemia)
  • Primary ciliary dyskinesia (Kartagener syndrome: bronchiectasis + situs inversus + infertility)
  • Allergic bronchopulmonary aspergillosis (ABPA)
  • Tumour/foreign body (localised)
  • Rheumatoid arthritis, IBD
💬 Features & Management
  • Chronic productive cough (large volumes of foul-smelling purulent sputum)
  • Dyspnoea, haemoptysis
  • Recurrent lower respiratory tract infections
  • Signs: coarse inspiratory crackles (basal > apical except TB-related); clubbing
  • Imaging: CXR: "tram-track" lines (thickened bronchial walls); CT: "signet ring" sign (airway > adjacent vessel)
  • Mx: Chest physiotherapy (airway clearance); nebulised hypertonic saline; antibiotics for exacerbations (cover Pseudomonas if colonised); long-term azithromycin (anti-inflammatory); bronchodilators; treat underlying cause
  • Complications: cor pulmonale, haemoptysis, secondary amyloidosis (AA)
⚡ High Yield
  • Hepatic hydrothorax: fluid is not coming from generalized pleural capillary leakage but from direct peritoneal–pleural transfer of ascitic fluid through defects in the diaphragm → predominantly right‑sided
  • CHF, nephrotic syndrome, and hypoalbuminaemia: primary issue is systemic elevation of hydrostatic pressure or reduction of oncotic pressure in the pulmonary/pleural microcirculation → bilateral
🧬 Causes
  • Idiopathic (IPF = UIP): most common; older men; TGF-β from injured pneumocytes → fibrosis; temporal heterogeneity on biopsy
  • Connective tissue disease: RA, SLE, systemic sclerosis, dermatomyositis
  • Drugs: amiodarone, bleomycin, methotrexate, nitrofurantoin, radiation
  • Pneumoconioses: asbestosis (lower lobe), silicosis (upper lobe, nodules), coal dust, berylliosis
  • Hypersensitivity pneumonitis: extrinsic allergic alveolitis (bird fancier's lung, farmer's lung)
  • Sarcoidosis: idiopathic granulomatous disease with extrapulmonary features (skin, eye, cardiac, etc.)
💬 Clinical Features
  • Progressive dyspnoea on exertion; dry non-productive cough
  • Bilateral fine inspiratory "Velcro" crackles (basal)
  • Decreased chest expansion
  • Clubbing
  • Signs of right HF (late)
  • Cyanosis (late)
🔬 Investigations
  • PFTs — Restrictive pattern: ↓FEV1, ↓↓FVC, FEV1/FVC normal or ↑; ↓TLC, ↓DLCO (gas transfer)
  • HRCT: IPF = bilateral subpleural, basal-predominant honeycombing ± traction bronchiectasis; "ground-glass" in NSIP
  • CXR: bilateral lower zone reticulonodular shadowing; "honeycomb lung" (end-stage)
  • ANA, ENA, RF (rheumatoid factor) — screen for CTD
  • Bronchoalveolar lavage/surgical lung biopsy if HRCT is not typical and diagnosis remains uncertain
💊 Management
  • IPF: Pirfenidone or Nintedanib (antifibrotic agents — slow progression; do not improve PF); lung transplant (definitive)
  • Treat underlying cause (stop offending drugs, treat CTD)
  • Pulmonary rehabilitation, O₂ (if hypoxic)
  • Prednisolone for hypersensitivity pneumonitis (acute); remove exposure
  • Prognosis: IPF — mean survival 4–6 years from diagnosis
⚡ High Yield
  • IPF shows UIP (usual interstitial pneumonia) histologic pattern: temporal heterogeneity = fibrosis of DIFFERENT ages on same biopsy; spatial heterogeneity = patchy fibrosis
  • Restrictive: ↓FEV1, ↓FVC, FEV1/FVC NORMAL or ↑ (contrast to obstructive where it's ↓)
  • Asbestosis: lower lobe fibrosis + pleural plaques; ↑risk of mesothelioma AND bronchogenic carcinoma (smoking synergistic)
  • Silicosis: upper lobe eggshell calcification of hilar nodes; ↑TB risk
🔬 Pathophysiology
  • Systemic granulomatous disease; non-caseating granulomas
  • Unknown antigen → CD4+ TH1 helper cell response → epithelioid granulomas
  • Granuloma macrophages produce 1α-hydroxylase → active vitamin D (1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D) → hypercalcaemia/hypercalciuria
  • Most common in young adults aged 20–40; highest incidence in people of African descent and Northern Europeans (Scandinavians); slight female predominance
  • Asteroid bodies within giant cells (characteristic stellate inclusions)
  • Negative tuberculin test (cutaneous anergy) despite normal T-cell function
💬 Multisystem Manifestations
  • Lung (most common): Bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy (BHL), restrictive lung disease, dry cough, dyspnoea
  • Eye: Anterior uveitis (painful red eye), lacrimal gland swelling, conjunctival nodules
  • Skin: Erythema nodosum (tender red nodules, shins — acute); lupus pernio (violaceous plaques on face — chronic); cutaneous nodules
  • Heart: Heart block, arrhythmias, cardiomyopathy
  • Neuro: Facial nerve palsy (VII), meningitis, diabetes insipidus
  • MSK: Polyarthritis, bone cysts (Perthes-Jüngling)
  • Heerfordt syndrome: uveitis + parotid swelling + facial palsy + fever
🔬 Investigations
  • CXR staging: 0=normal; I=BHL; II=BHL+parenchymal; III=parenchymal only; IV=fibrosis
  • ↑Serum ACE (not specific but useful for monitoring)
  • ↑Calcium (serum and urine)
  • ↑ESR, polyclonal hypergammaglobulinaemia
  • Negative Mantoux / IGRA (anergy)
  • Definitive: Biopsy showing non-caseating granulomas (exclude TB/fungal)
  • 24h urine calcium; ECG (heart block)
💊 Management
  • Often resolves spontaneously (stage I = 60–80% resolution)
  • Steroids (prednisolone): indicated for significant organ involvement (symptomatic lung, cardiac, neuro, uveitis, hypercalcaemia)
  • Steroid-sparing agents: methotrexate, azathioprine, hydroxychloroquine
  • Monitor with PFTs, CXR, ACE levels
  • Prognosis: most cases resolve within 2–5 years; 20% chronic/progressive
CDRIA Point Jon Lee — Sarcoidosis CXR Spot Diagnosis
  • Spot diagnosis clue: bulky hilar opacities on CXR are the classic sarcoidosis pattern in the right clinical setting.
  • Do not stop at the image: connect the CXR with symptoms and systemic features such as dry cough, exertional dyspnoea, eye symptoms, skin nodules, heart block/arrhythmias, or facial nerve palsy.
  • Practical safety check: exclude mimics such as TB or fungal infection before anchoring on sarcoidosis, especially if systemic symptoms or exposure risk are present.

🔬 Types of Chest Injury
  • Rib fractures: most common thoracic injury; localised pain + bony crepitus; splinting → atelectasis → pneumonia; rib 1–3 fractures → great vessel/brachial plexus injury; rib 10–12 → liver/spleen injury
  • Flail chest: ≥3 consecutive ribs fractured in ≥2 places → floating segment; paradoxical movement (segment moves IN on inspiration); hypoventilation → hypoxia → hypercapnia; LIFE-THREATENING
  • Pneumothorax: open (sucking chest wound) or closed
  • Haemothorax: blood in pleural space; dull to percussion; treat with chest drain
  • Pulmonary contusion: alveolar haemorrhage/oedema; worsens over 24–48h; treat supportively
  • Traumatic aortic injury: widened mediastinum on CXR; deceleration injury; CT aortogram
💊 Management
  • ATLS approach: ABCDE; manage life-threatening injuries first
  • Rib fractures: adequate analgesia (most important to prevent hypoventilation); NSAIDs, opioids, intercostal nerve block, epidural; incentive spirometry; physiotherapy
  • Flail chest: O₂; adequate analgesia; if severe → intubation + IPPV (intermittent positive pressure ventilation) + PEEP; surgical rib fixation may be considered
  • Open pneumothorax: three-sided occlusive dressing (flutter-valve); then formal chest drain
  • Tension pneumothorax: immediate needle decompression (do NOT wait for CXR)
  • Massive haemothorax (>1.5L or >200mL/hr): chest drain; surgical exploration
🧬 Aetiology
  • Pus in the pleural space
  • Causes: Parapneumonic (most common — complication of pneumonia), post-surgical, trauma, oesophageal rupture, haematogenous spread
  • Organisms: Streptococcus, Staph aureus, Gram-negatives (Klebsiella, E. coli), anaerobes
  • TB empyema (haemorrhagic)
  • Stages: exudative → fibropurulent → organising (fibrous peel formation)
💊 Features & Management
  • Fever persisting despite antibiotics; pleuritic chest pain; dyspnoea
  • Signs: dull to percussion, ↓breath sounds, constitutional symptoms
  • Pleural fluid: pH <7.2, glucose <3.3 mmol/L, ↑LDH, frank pus, positive culture
  • USS: loculated fluid; CT: pleural enhancement (split pleura sign)
  • Management: IV antibiotics; intercostal drain (ASAP if pH <7.2); intrapleural fibrinolytics (alteplase + DNase) for loculated; VATS (video-assisted thoracoscopic surgery) or surgical decortication if fails
  • Complications: trapped lung, bronchopleural fistula, sepsis
🔬 Pathophysiology
  • Autosomal recessive; CFTR (cystic fibrosis transmembrane conductance regulator) gene mutation
  • ΔF508 (delta-F508): most common mutation (70%); deletion of phenylalanine at position 508 → protein misfolding → degraded before reaching cell surface
  • Defective Cl⁻ channel → ↓Cl⁻ and water secretion → ↑Na⁺ and water absorption → thick, viscous secretions
  • Affects all exocrine glands: lungs, pancreas, intestine, vas deferens, sweat glands
  • Sweat glands: ↑Cl⁻ in sweat (can't reabsorb); diagnostic
💬 Clinical Features
  • Lung: Recurrent pulmonary infections (Staph aureus early; Pseudomonas aeruginosa later = poor prognosis); bronchiectasis; pneumothorax; haemoptysis; respiratory failure
  • GI: Meconium ileus (neonates — first presentation); pancreatic exocrine insufficiency → malabsorption, steatorrhoea, fat-soluble vitamin deficiency (A, D, E, K); CF-related diabetes; biliary cirrhosis
  • Reproductive: Male infertility (absent/obstructed vas deferens — congenital bilateral absence CBAVD)
  • Finger clubbing; failure to thrive; salty sweat (parents notice kissing)
🔬 Investigations
  • Sweat test: Cl⁻ >60 mmol/L (diagnostic); borderline 40–60
  • Newborn screening: immunoreactive trypsinogen (IRT) → then genetic testing
  • Genetic testing: confirm mutation (>2000 CFTR mutations known)
  • Spirometry: obstructive pattern (bronchiectasis); ↓FEV1/FVC
  • Sputum culture (monitor colonisation)
  • CXR/CT: bronchiectasis, hyperinflation, consolidation
💊 Management
  • Airway clearance: Chest physiotherapy (twice daily), nebulised hypertonic saline, nebulised dornase alfa (DNase — breaks down mucus)
  • Antibiotics: IV anti-pseudomonal (meropenem + colistin or tobramycin) for exacerbations; nebulised tobramycin/colistin for Pseudomonas colonisation; prophylactic azithromycin
  • CFTR modulators (precision medicine): Ivacaftor (CFTR potentiator, G551D mutation); Elexacaftor/Tezacaftor/Ivacaftor (Trikafta — works for ΔF508; approved >12yrs)
  • Pancreatic enzyme replacement (Creon with all meals); fat-soluble vitamins
  • Nutritional support (high calorie diet)
  • CF-related diabetes: insulin
  • Lung transplant (bilateral sequential): end-stage; median survival now >50 years with CFTR modulators
⚡ High Yield
  • ΔF508 = most common mutation; protein misfolding → not trafficked to cell surface

💬 Clinical Features
  • Acute hepatitis — 3 phases:
  • 1. Prodrome (1–2 wks): malaise, fatigue, anorexia, nausea, vomiting, RUQ discomfort, myalgia, low-grade fever; smokers often lose desire to smoke

💊 Management by Virus
  • Acute HAV/HEV: supportive — rest, hydration, avoid alcohol and hepatotoxic drugs; monitor INR; hospitalise if ALF signs
  • Acute HBV: mostly supportive; >95% of adults self-clear; antivirals (NRTI - chain-terminators) for severe acute HBV (INR >1.5 or high bilirubin)
  • Chronic HBV — when to treat: HBV DNA >2,000 IU/mL + ↑ALT OR significant fibrosis (Metavir ≥F2) OR HBeAg positive with high viral load
  • Chronic HBV — drugs: NRTI (Tenofovir (TDF/TAF) or Entecavir) — first-line; lifelong in cirrhosis; Pegylated interferon-α (finite course, selected cases)
  • Chronic HCV: Direct-acting antivirals (DAAs) — pangenotypic: sofosbuvir/velpatasvir (12 weeks); sofosbuvir/ledipasvir; elbasvir/grazoprevir (genotype 1/4); SVR (sustained virological response) = cure (>95%); monitor for reactivation of HBV if coinfected
  • Acute HCV: consider DAAs early — can prevent chronicity; monitor HCV RNA spontaneous clearance for 12–16 weeks first
  • Post-exposure prophylaxis (HBV): HBIg (hepatitis B immunoglobulin) + vaccine if non-immune and exposed to HBsAg-positive blood
  • Vaccination: HAV (2 doses), HBV (3 doses — 0, 1, 6 months); both recommended for healthcare workers, travellers
  • All hepatitis = notifiable disease; contact tracing; screen close contacts
🔬 Investigations
  • First-line bloods: LFTs (ALT, AST, ALP, GGT, bilirubin, albumin), INR/PT, FBC, UECr
  • Serology (gold top/SST tube — sent to serology lab; result 24–72 hrs):
  • Suspected HAV: anti-HAV IgM (acute); IgG (past/vaccinated)
  • Suspected HBV: HBsAg, anti-HBs, anti-HBc (IgM + IgG), HBeAg, anti-HBe, HBV DNA (quantitative PCR)
  • Suspected HCV: anti-HCV antibody (screening); HCV RNA by PCR (confirms active infection — antibody can be negative early); HCV genotype (treatment selection)
  • Suspected HEV: anti-HEV IgM/IgG; HEV RNA (if immunocompromised)
  • Liver imaging: USS (size, echogenicity, portal vein, splenomegaly)
  • Liver biopsy / Fibroscan: assess fibrosis stage in chronic hepatitis
  • Complication screen in chronic: elevated AFP (alpha-fetoprotein) + USS every 6 months (HCC); gastroscopy (varices if cirrhosis)

🔬 Complications & Progression
  • Chronic hepatitis → fibrosis → cirrhosis → HCC: rate depends on virus and host factors
  • HBV fibrosis rate: ~20% develop cirrhosis over 20 years; HCC can occur even without cirrhosis
  • HCV fibrosis rate: ~20% develop cirrhosis over 20–30 years; cofactors accelerate (alcohol, obesity, HIV coinfection)
  • Acute liver failure (ALF): HAV (rare, <0.5%), HBV (1%), HEV in pregnancy (~20%); requires ICU; liver transplant evaluation
  • Fibroscan/biopsy staging (Metavir): F0 = none; F1 = mild; F2 = significant; F3 = severe; F4 = cirrhosis
  • HCV SVR: achieving sustained virological response reduces HCC risk by ~75% and all-cause mortality; does NOT eliminate risk in those with established cirrhosis
  • HBV reactivation can occur during immunosuppression (chemotherapy, biologics, steroids) — screen all before starting
⚡ High Yield
  • "Naked viruses (HAV + HEV) lack envelope — not destroyed by gut — the vowels hit your bowels " (faecal-oral transmission)
  • HCV: no 3′–5′ exonuclease → no proofreading → antigenic variation → no vaccine; cured by DAAs
  • HEV in pregnancy = fulminant hepatic failure (~20% mortality); think HEV in returning traveller with jaundice in 3rd trimester
  • Window period: HBsAg disappears but anti-HBs not yet appeared → ONLY anti-HBc IgM is positive
  • Vaccinated: anti-HBs IgG ONLY positive (no anti-HBc — never naturally infected)
  • Neonatal HBV → 90% chronic (immature immune tolerance); adult HBV → 90% resolve (competent immune response clears virus)
  • HDV superinfection (HDV infects someone with chronic HBV) = worse than co-infection (both acquired simultaneously)
  • HBV can cause HCC WITHOUT cirrhosis — unique among hepatitis viruses (direct oncogenic integration into host genome)
  • Most patients with chronic hepatitis are ASYMPOTMATIC. Symptoms develop with the onset of cirrhosis and decompensated liver disease (eg. ascites, gastro-intestinal bleeding).
  • Serology rule: IgM = recent/active infection; IgG = past infection or vaccination; applies to HAV, HBV, HEV
CDRIA Point Jon Lee — Serology Interpretation for Hepatitis
  • Serology is used to detect viruses from a blood sample (gold top / SST tube — sent separately to serology/microbiology lab; takes 24–72 hours)
  • Universal rule: IgM = recent or ACTIVE infection; IgG = evidence of PAST infection or prior immunisation/vaccination
  • Example (HAV): Hepatitis A IgG detected + IgM not detected = past infection or immunisation — NOT active hepatitis A
  • HBV serology requires interpreting multiple markers together — not just one result. Pattern recognition is key: HBsAg + Anti-HBc IgM = acute. HBsAg only + Anti-HBc IgG = chronic. Anti-HBs only = vaccinated
  • HCV serology: Anti-HCV antibody positive = exposure (past or present); HCV RNA PCR positive = active infection. Antibody can be transiently negative early in acute infection (window)
  • Clinical correlation always: Serology results must be interpreted in the context of clinical presentation, LFT pattern, and risk factors

Chronic Liver Disease

🧬 Aetiology
  • End-stage liver disease (irreversible and advanced stage of chronic liver disease): diffuse bridging fibrosis + regenerative nodules disrupting normal architecture. Fibrosis mediated by TGF-β from hepatic stellate cells
  • Stellate cells → Myofibroblasts → Fibrosis
🍺 Toxic / Metabolic
Alcohol — most common in developed world (Australia/UK); >80g/day for ≥10 years
NAFLD/NASH — fastest growing cause globally; driven by obesity, T2DM, metabolic syndrome
Drugs — methotrexate (dose-dependent), amiodarone, isoniazid, nitrofurantoin
🦠 Viral
Chronic HCV — most common viral cause of cirrhosis in Australia/Western world; ~20–30yr progression
Chronic HBV — most common viral cause of cirrhosis worldwide; unique: can cause HCC without cirrhosis
🛡️ Autoimmune / Biliary
Autoimmune hepatitis (AIH) — anti-smooth muscle Ab (type 1); young/middle-aged women; responds to steroids
Primary biliary cholangitis (PBC) — anti-mitochondrial Ab (AMA); middle-aged women; pruritus; UDCA
Primary sclerosing cholangitis (PSC) — pANCA; middle-aged men + UC; beaded bile ducts on MRCP; no proven medical therapy
🧬 Genetic / Metabolic
Haemochromatosis (HFE mutation) — iron overload → "bronze diabetes"; treat with phlebotomy
Wilson disease (ATP7B mutation) — copper accumulation → Kayser-Fleischer rings; <40yrs; penicillamine
α1-antitrypsin deficiency (PiZZ) — misfolded protein in hepatocytes + emphysema (lower lobes); young non-smoker
Glycogen storage diseases, galactosaemia — rare; present in childhood
❓ Other / Unknown
Cryptogenic — ~10%; diagnosis of exclusion after full workup (often occult NAFLD or burnt-out AIH)
Cardiac cirrhosis — chronic right heart failure / constrictive pericarditis → "nutmeg liver" → centrilobular fibrosis
Budd-Chiari syndrome — hepatic vein thrombosis → congestive hepatopathy → cirrhosis
💬 Clinical Features
  • Portal hypertension: ascites, oesophageal/gastric varices, caput medusae, splenomegaly, haemorrhoids
    • Hepatic venous pressure gradient (HVPG) is an indirect measure of portal vein pressure (normal ≤5 mmHg)
    • Clinically significant portal hypertension is defined as an increase in HVPG to >10 mmHg
  • Impaired detoxification: hepatic encephalopathy (↑NH₃ → asterixis, confusion, coma), jaundice, fetor hepaticus
  • Impaired synthesis: hypoalbuminaemia (oedema), coagulopathy (↑PT/INR), thrombocytopaenia
  • Hyperoestrogenism (impaired oestrogen metabolism): spider naevi, palmar erythema, gynaecomastia, testicular atrophy, amenorrhoea
  • Late complications: hepatorenal syndrome, spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (SBP), HCC
  • Hepatic encephalopathy grades: I (mild confusion) → II (asterixis, disorientation) → III (somnolent, incoherent) → IV (coma)
  • Muslce wasting, spelnomegaly, spider naevi, testicular atrophy
🔬 Investigations
  • LFTs: ↑bilirubin, ↑ALT/AST (reduced in end-stage — "used up"), ↑GGT/ALP, ↓albumin, ↑PT
  • Diagnostic markers: Platelets, INR, and Albumin; ALT/AST can be low/normal in cirrhosis (even in advanced disease)
  • FBC: thrombocytopaenia, anaemia (multifactorial)
  • USS liver: nodular liver, splenomegaly, ascites, portal vein dilation
  • Liver biopsy: gold standard — bridging fibrosis + regenerative nodules (Metavir/Ishak score); BUT not often required → can be used to diagnos aetiology of liver disease of where it is necessary to determine fibrosis/cirrhosis
  • Fibroscan (transient elastography): non-invasive liver stiffness measurement
  • Scoring for Cirrhosis
    • Child-Pugh score (based on 5 lab criteria: bilirubin, albumin, PT, ascites, encephalopathy) — assess severity
    • MELD score (bilirubin, creatinine, INR) — predicts 3-month mortality; used for transplant listing
  • AFP + USS every 6 months: HCC surveillance in cirrhosis
💊 Management
  • Treat underlying cause (stop alcohol, antivirals for HBV/HCV, immunosuppression for autoimmune)
  • Ascites: low-sodium diet, spironolactone ± furosemide; large-volume paracentesis (LVP) + IV albumin for tense ascites; TIPS for refractory
  • Varices: non-selective β-blocker (propranolol/carvedilol) for prophylaxis; endoscopic band ligation (EBL) for active bleeding; terlipressin + EBL acutely; TIPS
  • Encephalopathy: lactulose (↓NH₄⁺ generation); rifaximin (↓NH₃-producing gut bacteria); identify and treat precipitants (GI bleed, infection, constipation, renal failure)
  • SBP: diagnostic paracentesis (ANC >250 cells/mm³ = SBP); ceftriaxone; prophylaxis with norfloxacin
  • Liver transplantation: definitive treatment; MELD ≥15 or decompensated cirrhosis

🦠 Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis (SBP) — Expanded
  • SBP is infection of ascitic fluid in the absence of an intra-abdominal surgically treatable cause (e.g. intra-abdominal sepsis)
  • It occurs almost exclusively in patients with cirrhosis and ascites
  • Symptoms include fever, abdominal pain and tenderness
  • High-Risk Groups — Antibiotic Prophylaxis Indicated
    1. Cirrhosis + active GI bleeding
    2. Previous episode of SBP — recurrence rate ~70% within one year
    3. Cirrhosis + ascites with ascitic fluid protein <15 g/L AND at least one of: impaired renal function or liver failure
  • Prophylactic antibiotic options: Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole; Ciprofloxacin; Norfloxacin
⚡ High Yield

  • Hepatic encephalopathy is metabolic (reversible) — ammonia neurotoxicity; treat with lactulose + rifaximin
  • Asterixis ("flapping tremor") = hepatic encephalopathy hallmark
  • SBP — gram-negative organisms most common (E. coli, Klebsiella); empiric 3rd gen cephalosporin
  • All cirrhotics need: 6-monthly USS + AFP (HCC surveillance), variceal screening endoscopy, vaccination (HAV, HBV, pneumococcal, influenza)
CDRIA Point Jon Lee — LFT Pattern Recognition
  • LFT interpretation — patterns (not individual values): Always compare with baseline. "Lots of things can mess with LFTs — clinical correlation and trends are important. If you see abnormal LFTs — check old ones!"
  • Pattern 1 — Cholestatic/Obstructive: Predominant ↑ALP + ↑GGT, ↑bilirubin → think gallstones, pancreatic mass causing bile duct obstruction, PSC, PBC
  • Pattern 2 — Hepatitic/Hepatocellular: Predominantly ↑ALT + ↑GGT (transaminases) → think hepatitis (viral, drug-induced, autoimmune)
  • Pattern 3 — Alcoholic: Predominantly isolated ↑GGT → think alcoholic hepatitis/alcoholism. AST:ALT ratio >2:1 is typical of alcoholic liver disease
  • Pattern 4 — Mixed: All elevated to a similar degree → hepatitis or cirrhosis with mixed picture
  • Pattern 5 — Isolated ↑Bilirubin: Think Gilbert's syndrome (benign, triggered by fasting/illness/stress) — not clinically important
  • Key rule: "Sometimes a hepatitis can have a cholestatic-looking picture or an obstruction can show quite high transaminases — patterns are just a guide. Clinical correlation always!"

🔬 Pathological Spectrum
  • [1] Hepatic steatosis (fatty change): macrovesicular fat; most common and earliest change; reversible with abstinence; usually asymptomatic; occurs after even moderate alcohol intake
  • [2] Alcoholic hepatitis (AH): requires sustained heavy drinking; hepatocyte ballooning and necrosis + neutrophilic infiltration; Mallory-Denk bodies (intracytoplasmic eosinophilic inclusions of ubiquitinated keratin aggregates); perivenular fibrosis; can be superimposed on cirrhosis
  • [3] Alcoholic cirrhosis: final irreversible stage; micronodular → macronodular pattern; regenerative nodules + fibrous bands → portal hypertension → decompensation; HCC risk elevated even without other risk factors
  • Spectrum is not linear: patients may present with cirrhosis without prior clinical hepatitis; all stages can co-exist
⚠️ Risk Factors
  • Dose-dependent: >21 standard drinks/week (men), >14/week (women) — no safe threshold for liver disease
  • Pattern of drinking: binge drinking and drinking outside of meals increases risk beyond total volume
  • Sex: women develop ALD at lower doses and faster — lower ADH activity, higher fat:water ratio → higher BAC per drink
  • Genetic: PNPLA3 I148M polymorphism — strongest genetic risk factor for ALD severity and HCC
  • Metabolic co-factors: obesity and type 2 diabetes markedly accelerate fibrosis progression
  • Concurrent hepatitis: HBV/HCV co-infection synergistically accelerates cirrhosis
  • Nutrition: malnutrition is nearly universal in severe AH; worsens outcomes independently
💬 Clinical Features by Stage
  • Steatosis: usually asymptomatic; soft hepatomegaly; ↑GGT ± ↑ALT; incidental on imaging
  • Alcoholic hepatitis (mild–moderate): fatigue, anorexia, RUQ discomfort, low-grade fever; jaundice may be absent
  • Severe alcoholic hepatitis: deep jaundice, fever, tender hepatomegaly, leukocytosis, coagulopathy; high short-term mortality (28-day); encephalopathy indicates very severe disease
  • Compensated cirrhosis: often clinically silent; spider naevi, palmar erythema, Dupuytren's, parotid enlargement, gynaecomastia, testicular atrophy
  • Decompensated cirrhosis: ascites, variceal bleeding, hepatic encephalopathy, jaundice, spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (SBP), hepatorenal syndrome (HRS)
🧪 Investigations
  • LFTs: AST:ALT >2:1 (AST rarely >500 U/L) reflecting reduced hepatic ALT activity due to pyridoxal‑5‑phosphate (vitamin B6) deficiency; ↑GGT (most sensitive alcohol marker); ↑bilirubin in AH
  • FBE: macrocytosis (MCV >100), thrombocytopenia (hypersplenism), anaemia
  • Coagulation: ↑INR (↓hepatic synthetic function) — use to calculate Maddrey DF
  • Maddrey Discriminant Function (MDF): 4.6 × (PT − control) + bilirubin (μmol/L ÷ 17); MDF ≥32 = severe AH → consider corticosteroids
  • MELD score: now preferred for prognosis; MELD >20 correlates with high 90-day mortality in AH
  • Imaging: USS — fatty liver (echogenic), nodular contour in cirrhosis, splenomegaly, ascites; Fibroscan (elastography) to stage fibrosis
  • Liver biopsy: not always required; confirms diagnosis if uncertain; important before transplant listing in equivocal cases
  • AFP: screen for HCC every 6 months in cirrhosis (USS ± AFP)
💊 Management
  • Cornerstone — abstinence: the single most effective intervention at all stages; even in decompensated cirrhosis, sustained abstinence can improve portal pressure and Child-Pugh class
  • Nutritional support: 35–40 kcal/kg/day, 1.2–1.5g protein/kg/day; enteral feeding if unable to meet needs orally; thiamine IV before any glucose (prevent Wernicke's); folate, B12, zinc, magnesium supplementation
  • Mild–moderate AH: abstinence + nutrition; treat withdrawal (see AUD); supportive care
  • Severe AH (MDF ≥32 or MELD >20):
    • Prednisolone 40mg/day × 28 days — assess response at Day 7 using Lille score (>0.45 = non-responder → stop steroids); improves 28-day survival in responders
    • Contraindications to steroids: active infection (must treat first), active GI bleeding, hepatorenal syndrome, renal failure
    • Pentoxifylline: no longer recommended as monotherapy (STOPAH trial); no survival benefit over prednisolone
    • N-acetylcysteine: may be used as adjunct with prednisolone in severe AH (reduces infection risk, may improve 1-month survival)
  • Cirrhosis management: manage portal hypertension (propranolol/carvedilol for varices prophylaxis; EVL), diuretics for ascites (spironolactone ± frusemide), lactulose/rifaximin for encephalopathy, SBP prophylaxis (norfloxacin or co-trimoxazole)
  • Liver transplantation: traditional 6-month abstinence rule; Early liver transplant increasingly considered for carefully selected patients with severe AH non-responding to steroids (Mathurin criteria) — shown to improve 2-year survival; requires multidisciplinary psychosocial assessment; not universally available in Australia
  • Emerging therapies: IL-1 receptor antagonist (anakinra + zinc + N-acetylcysteine — DASH trial data), gut microbiome modulation, FXR agonists — currently investigational

🧬 Pathophysiology
  • Spectrum: NAFLD (steatosis without significant inflammation) → NASH (non-alcoholic steatohepatitis; steatosis + lobular inflammation + hepatocyte ballooning) → fibrosis → cirrhosis → HCC
  • Strongly associated with metabolic syndrome : obesity (most important), insulin resistance/T2DM, hypertension, hypertriglyceridaemia, ↓HDL
  • Mechanism: insulin resistance → ↑free fatty acid delivery to liver → hepatic fat accumulation → oxidative stress → inflammation (NASH)
  • Now renamed MASLD (metabolic dysfunction-associated steatotic liver disease) in newer guidelines
  • Rapidly becoming the most common cause of chronic liver disease globally
💬 Clinical Features, Ix & Mx
  • Usually asymptomatic ; incidentally found on USS or ↑ALT/AST on bloods
  • Fatigue, RUQ discomfort; hepatomegaly
  • Investigations: LFTs (↑ALT > AST, ↑GGT); USS (echogenic "bright" liver); Fibroscan (stiffness); liver biopsy — gold standard (NAS score for NASH)
  • Exclude other causes: alcohol history, viral hepatitis serology, autoimmune, metabolic screen
  • Management: weight loss (most effective — 7–10% weight reduction can resolve NASH); treat metabolic syndrome (DM, HTN, dyslipidaemia); Mediterranean diet; exercise; bariatric surgery for morbid obesity
  • Pharmacological: resmetirom (THR-β agonist) — FDA approved 2024 for NASH with fibrosis; GLP-1 agonists (semaglutide) emerging evidence; pioglitazone (T2DM + NASH)
  • HCC surveillance if advanced fibrosis/cirrhosis
⚡ High Yield
  • NAFLD/NASH can progress to cirrhosis and HCC even in the absence of alcohol
  • Histology of NASH mimics alcoholic hepatitis (steatosis + Mallory bodies + ballooning) — distinguish by history
  • ALT > AST in NAFLD (opposite to alcoholic liver disease where AST > ALT)
  • Weight loss of ≥7–10% is the single most effective intervention
🧬 Classification & Causes
  • Definition: hepatic venous pressure gradient (HVPG) >5 mmHg (normal 1–5 mmHg); clinically significant >10 mmHg → varices
  • Pre-hepatic: portal vein thrombosis, splenic vein thrombosis; liver function normal
  • Hepatic (intrahepatic — most common): cirrhosis (most common cause in developed world), schistosomiasis (most common worldwide), hepatic vein obstruction (Budd-Chiari)
  • Post-hepatic: Budd-Chiari syndrome (hepatic vein thrombosis), right heart failure, constrictive pericarditis
  • Budd-Chiari syndrome: hepatic venous outflow obstruction → hepatomegaly, ascites, RUQ pain, liver failure; associated with polycythaemia vera, hypercoagulable states, HCC, pregnancy
⚠️ Consequences & Management
  • Portosystemic shunts (varices): oesophageal varices (90% of cirrhotics at 10 years; haematemesis/melaena), gastric varices, rectal varices (haemorrhoids), caput medusae (periumbilical)
  • Ascites: SAAG ≥1.1; treat with diuretics (spironolactone + furosemide), paracentesis, TIPS
  • Splenomegaly → hypersplenism: thrombocytopaenia, anaemia, leukopenia
  • Acute variceal bleeding: resuscitation; terlipressin (↓splanchnic blood flow) + IV antibiotics (norfloxacin/ceftriaxone); urgent endoscopy (EBL/sclerotherapy); TIPS if uncontrolled; Sengstaken-Blakemore tube as bridge
  • Primary prophylaxis: non-selective β-blocker (propranolol) OR EBL for medium/large varices
  • Secondary prophylaxis: β-blocker + EBL combination
  • TIPS (Transjugular Intrahepatic Portosystemic Shunt): reduces portal pressure; used for refractory ascites and variceal bleeding; can worsen encephalopathy

  • Variceal bleeding = medical emergency; terlipressin + endoscopic band ligation is standard first-line
  • Budd-Chiari = hepatic vein thrombosis; triad: hepatomegaly + ascites + RUQ pain; NO JVD (distinguishes from right HF); nutmeg liver
  • TIPS reduces portal hypertension but increases risk of hepatic encephalopathy (bypasses liver)

Gallbladder Disease

🧬 Types & Risk Factors
  • Cholesterol stones (80%): radiolucent; ↑cholesterol + ↓bile salts + gallbladder stasis → supersaturation → crystallisation
  • 7 F's risk factors: Female, Fat (obese), Fertile (multiparity), Forty (age), Fair (Caucasian/Native American), Feeds (TPN), Fasting (rapid weight loss)
  • Also: Crohn disease, oestrogen therapy, fibrates, hyperlipidaemia
  • Pigment stones (20%): calcium bilirubinate; black = radiopaque (chronic haemolysis, alcoholic cirrhosis); brown = radiolucent (biliary infection/stasis)
  • Brown pigment stones: biliary tract infection, Clonorchis sinensis (liver fluke)
💬 Clinical Spectrum
  • Asymptomatic (silent stones): 80%; incidental USS finding; manage conservatively
  • Biliary colic: episodic RUQ/epigastric pain after fatty meals (CCK → gallbladder contraction → stone in cystic duct); nausea/vomiting; resolves within hours; labs normal; USS shows cholelithiasis
  • Cholecystitis: persistent stone impaction in cystic duct → inflammation → fever, RUQ tenderness, Murphy's sign positive (inspiratory arrest on RUQ palpation); ↑WCC, ↑CRP; USS: gallbladder wall thickening, pericholecystic fluid
  • Choledocholithiasis: stone in CBD → ↑ALP, ↑GGT, ↑direct bilirubin, jaundice; USS/MRCP; manage with ERCP
  • Acute cholangitis: CBD obstruction + infection → Charcot's triad: fever/rigors + jaundice + RUQ pain; Reynolds pentad (+ confusion + hypotension) = septic cholangitis → emergency ERCP
  • Acute pancreatitis: stone at ampulla of Vater → pancreatic duct obstruction
  • Gallstone ileus: fistula to GI tract → stone enters bowel → obstruction at ileocaecal valve; Rigler's triad on CXR/AXR: pneumobilia + SBO + ectopic gallstone
🔬 Investigations
  • USS (first-line): first-line; excellent for gallstones and cholecystitis; echogenic foci with acoustic shadowing
  • ERCP: diagnostic + therapeutic (in case of acute cholangitis) → stone extraction, sphincterotomy, stenting; risk of post-ERCP pancreatitis
  • MRCP: best non-invasive test for CBD stones and biliary tree anatomy only if you have time and uncertainty
  • HIDA scan: if USS inconclusive; failure to visualise gallbladder = cystic duct obstruction (cholecystitis)
  • CT abdomen: complications (perforation, abscess, pancreatitis)
  • LFTs, lipase/amylase, FBC, CRP, blood cultures (MCS)
💊 Management
  • Biliary colic: elective laparoscopic cholecystectomy if symptomatic; analgesia (NSAIDs/opioids), antispasmodics acutely
  • Acute cholecystitis: IV fluids, analgesia, IV antibiotics; laparoscopic cholecystectomy within 72 hours (early) or interval 6 weeks (if unfit for early surgery); percutaneous cholecystostomy if unfit for surgery
  • CBD stones: ERCP + sphincterotomy + stone extraction → followed by cholecystectomy
  • Acute cholangitis: IV antibiotics + urgent ERCP (ideally within 12–24h for severe/septic cases; within 48–72h for moderate cases))
  • Acalculous cholecystitis (critically ill patients): percutaneous cholecystostomy
  • Porcelain gallbladder: prophylactic cholecystectomy (↑risk adenocarcinoma)
  • IV Antibiotics: piperacillin + tazobactam OR ceftriaxone + metronidazole (if severe penicillin allergy)
⚡ High Yield
  • Biliary colic risk factors: hx of gallstones; female > male; obesity; sudden weight loss; late pregnancy or recently delivered
  • Charcot's triad = fever + RUQ pain + jaundice → acute cholangitis
  • Reynolds pentad = Charcot's triad + confusion + hypotension → septic cholangitis → emergency ERCP
  • Murphy's sign = inspiratory arrest on RUQ deep palpation = acute cholecystitis
  • Rigler's triad = pneumobilia + small bowel obstruction + ectopic radiopaque gallstone = gallstone ileus
  • Courvoisier's law: palpable, non-tender gallbladder + jaundice = malignant biliary obstruction (e.g. pancreatic head cancer), NOT gallstones (gallstones → small, fibrotic, non-distensible gallbladder)
  • Cholesterol stones = radiolucent; black pigment stones = radiopaque

Liver Tumours

🧬 Aetiology & Risk Factors
  • HCC is among the top 5 most common cancers and a leading cause of cancer death worldwide
  • Most common liver tumour overall = metastases (primarily GI especially colorectal, then pancreas, stomach, breast, lung)
  • Risk factors: cirrhosis from any cause (80–90% of HCC arises in cirrhotic liver)
  • HBV (± cirrhosis — can cause HCC without cirrhosis), HCV, alcoholic cirrhosis, NASH-cirrhosis, haemochromatosis, Wilson disease, α1-antitrypsin deficiency
  • Aflatoxin B1 (Aspergillus contamination of grains/peanuts) — specific carcinogen; causes TP53 mutations
  • Most common benign liver tumour = cavernous haemangioma (do NOT biopsy — haemorrhage risk)
💬 Presentation, Diagnosis & Management
  • Symptoms: often asymptomatic (detected on surveillance); anorexia, weight loss, RUQ pain, jaundice, tender hepatomegaly; sudden decompensation of previously stable cirrhosis
  • Portal vein thrombosis (tumour thrombus); haemoperitoneum (rupture)
  • Diagnosis: USS + AFP for screening (6-monthly in cirrhosis); contrast CT or MRI (arterial enhancement + washout = characteristic); biopsy if diagnosis uncertain
  • AFP (alpha-fetoprotein): elevated in ~70% of HCC; also raised in germ cell tumours, pregnancy, regenerating liver
  • BCLC staging: guides treatment (Barcelona Clinic Liver Cancer)
  • Treatment: curative — surgical resection or liver transplantation (Milan criteria: 1 lesion ≤5cm or ≤3 lesions ≤3cm each); local ablation (RFA, microwave); bridging/downstaging — TACE (transarterial chemoembolisation); systemic — sorafenib/lenvatinib (tyrosine kinase inhibitors); atezolizumab + bevacizumab (immunotherapy; 1st line advanced)
  • 5-year survival: <20% overall due to late presentation
⚡ High Yield
  • HCC almost always arises in cirrhosis — any cause. HBV is the exception (can cause HCC without cirrhosis)
  • AFP + USS every 6 months = surveillance protocol for all cirrhotics
  • Contrast CT/MRI hallmark: arterial hyperenhancement + portal venous washout ("filling in then washing out")
  • Milan criteria for liver transplant: 1 lesion ≤5cm, or up to 3 lesions each ≤3cm, no vascular invasion, no extrahepatic spread
  • Aflatoxin B1 = hepatotoxic carcinogen → TP53 mutation → HCC in non-cirrhotic liver

Gastrointestinal System

Diseases of the Oesophagus, Stomach & Duodenum

🔬 Pathophysiology & Causes
  • Transient LOS relaxations → acid reflux → oesophageal mucosal injury
  • Risk factors: obesity (most important), hiatus hernia, pregnancy, fatty/spicy food, alcohol, smoking, caffeine; drugs: CCBs, nitrates, anticholinergics (all ↓LOS tone)
  • Barrett's oesophagus: chronic acid → squamous → intestinal columnar metaplasia (goblet cells); ↑risk adenocarcinoma (~0.3%/yr); requires surveillance
  • Complications: erosive oesophagitis (LA grades A–D), peptic stricture, Barrett's, adenocarcinoma
💬 Symptoms & Signs
  • Typical: heartburn (retrosternal burning, worse post-meals/lying), acid regurgitation, water brash; relieved by antacids/sitting up
  • Atypical: chronic cough, hoarseness (laryngopharyngeal reflux), asthma, dental erosions, globus sensation
  • Dysphagia if stricture (solids > liquids)
  • Alarm features → urgent OGD: dysphagia, odynophagia, weight loss, haematemesis/melaena, iron-deficiency anaemia, persistent vomiting, age ≥50 new onset
🔬 Investigations
  • Typical symptoms, no alarm features: clinical diagnosis → empirical PPI trial 4–8 weeks
  • OGD: if alarm features, treatment failure, or pre-surgical; assess oesophagitis grade, Barrett's, biopsy
  • 24-hr pH ± impedance monitoring: gold standard; reserved for atypical symptoms or pre-fundoplication
  • Oesophageal manometry: pre-fundoplication; exclude achalasia (contraindicated if found)
💊 Management
  • Lifestyle (always first): weight loss, HOB elevation, avoid triggers, small meals, no eating <3h before bed
  • PRN symptom relief: Gaviscon (alginate raft), antacids
  • PPI (first-line): omeprazole 20mg / pantoprazole 40mg OD, 30 min before first meal; 4–8 weeks; step-down or on-demand long term
  • H₂ blockers: famotidine — if PPI not tolerated
  • Barrett's: long-term PPI; surveillance OGD (2–5-yearly no dysplasia; 6-monthly low-grade dysplasia; EMR + RFA for high-grade/early carcinoma)
  • Surgery: laparoscopic Nissen fundoplication for refractory GORD or large hiatus hernia (requires pre-op pH + manometry)
⚡ High Yield
  • Barrett's: goblet cell intestinal metaplasia → adenocarcinoma risk ~0.3%/yr; RFA for dysplasia
  • Alarm features → always OGD first; never empirical PPI alone
  • Obesity = most important modifiable risk factor — weight loss may fully resolve GORD
  • Fundoplication contraindicated in achalasia (confirmed on manometry first)
  • Stop PPIs ≥2 weeks before UBT or stool antigen (H. pylori) to avoid false negatives
  • Eosinophilic oesophagitis (EoE): mimic in young adults; food impaction; rings/furrows on OGD; >15 eosinophils/HPF on biopsy; treat with swallowed fluticasone or dietary elimination

  • E thanol/Alcohol (35% in Australia) — toxic to acinar cells; precipitates protein plugs
  • T rauma (blunt abdominal trauma, post-ERCP — most common iatrogenic cause)
  • S teroids (high-dose)
  • M umps (paramyxovirus; other viruses: CMV, EBV, Coxsackie)
  • A utoimmune (IgG4-related; type 1 AIP — responds to steroids; sausage-shaped pancreas)
  • S corpion sting (Tityus species — rare but classic)
  • H ypercalcaemia / Hypertriglyceridaemia (>11 mmol/L or >1000 mg/dL)
  • E RCP (~3–5% of ERCPs); also ERCP-related pancreatitis reduced by rectal indomethacin prophylaxis
  • D rugs: azathioprine, thiazides, furosemide, sulfa drugs, NRTIs, valproate, didanosine, tetracyclines
🔬 Pathophysiology
  • Premature activation of trypsinogen → trypsin within acinar cells → autodigestion of pancreas
  • Phospholipase A2 → fat necrosis (saponification — calcium soap deposition → hypocalcaemia)
  • Elastase → vessel wall digestion → haemorrhage
  • Local: oedema, fat necrosis, acinar cell necrosis (severe)
  • Systemic: SIRS → ARDS, AKI, DIC
  • Grey Turner's sign: flank bruising (haemorrhagic pancreatitis)
  • Cullen's sign: periumbilical bruising (haemorrhagic pancreatitis)
💬 Clinical Features
  • Sudden-onset severe epigastric/central abdominal pain radiating to the back ("boring through")
  • Nausea, vomiting (doesn't relieve pain)
  • Low-grade fever, tachycardia
  • Epigastric tenderness; guarding in severe disease
  • Jaundice if gallstone impacted at ampulla of Vater (double duct sign)
  • Signs of haemorrhagic: Grey Turner's (flanks), Cullen's (umbilicus), Fox's sign (inguinal)
🔬 Diagnosis (Revised Atlanta — 2 of 3 criteria)
  • 1. Characteristic abdominal pain
  • 2. Serum lipase or amylase >3× upper limit of normal (lipase preferred — more specific, stays elevated longer)
  • 3. Characteristic findings on CT/MRI
  • Bloods: FBC (leukocytosis), U&E, glucose, calcium, LFTs (↑ALP/GGT if gallstone), LDH, albumin
  • USS abdomen: within 24h — identify gallstones (bile duct dilation)
  • CT abdomen with IV contrast (CECT): gold standard for severity staging; DO NOT do on admission unless diagnostic uncertainty or clinical deterioration; best 48–72h after onset
  • Severity scoring: Revised Atlanta (mild/moderately severe/severe); BISAP score; Ranson criteria; APACHE-II
⚠️ Complications
  • Local: Acute peripancreatic fluid collection → pancreatic pseudocyst (lined by granulation tissue, NOT epithelium — forms >4 weeks); pancreatic necrosis; walled-off necrosis (WON)
  • Systemic (severe): ARDS (most feared); AKI; DIC; sepsis; multi-organ failure
  • Infected necrosis: fever + deterioration + gas in necrosis on CT → empiric antibiotics (meropenem); endoscopic/surgical necrosectomy
  • Hypocalcaemia (Ca²⁺ soaps), hyperglycaemia, hypoalbuminaemia
  • Colonic ischaemia (splenic flexure watershed)
💊 Management
  • Aggressive IV fluid resuscitation: Hartmann's (lactated Ringer's) preferred — shown to reduce SIRS vs normal saline; 250–500 mL/hr initially
  • Analgesia: IV morphine acceptable (historical concern about sphincter of Oddi spasm not clinically significant); patient-controlled analgesia; avoid NSAIDs (if renal compromise)
  • Nutrition: Early enteral nutrition (nasojejunal/nasogastric feed within 24–48h for severe) is superior to parenteral nutrition; "gut rest" (NBM) reserved for first 24–48h in mild disease
  • Monitor: hourly urine output, daily bloods, fluid balance
  • Antibiotics: NOT routine; only if infected necrosis confirmed (IV imipenem or meropenem)
  • Gallstone pancreatitis: Urgent ERCP + sphincterotomy if cholangitis or persistent CBD stone; laparoscopic cholecystectomy during same admission (mild) or after recovery (severe)
  • Hypertriglyceridaemia pancreatitis: Insulin infusion ± plasmapheresis; fibrates long-term
  • ICU/HDU for severe; consider surgical/endoscopic necrosectomy if infected necrosis
⚡ High Yield
  • Lipase >3× ULN preferred over amylase (more specific; stays elevated longer)
  • Gallstones = most common cause in Australia (40%); Alcohol = 2nd (35%)
  • Hypocalcaemia: Ca²⁺ binds to free fatty acids (saponification) → stones. NOT due to ↑PTH
  • Pseudocyst: lined by granulation tissue (NOT epithelium) — distinguish from true cyst
  • Hartmann's/Ringer's lactate > normal saline for fluid resuscitation in pancreatitis
  • CECT not needed on admission unless diagnostic uncertainty; best at 48–72h to assess necrosis
  • Early enteral feeding (within 24–48h) superior to TPN in severe pancreatitis
🧬 Aetiology & Pathophysiology
  • Alcohol (70%): most common in adults; direct toxic effect on acinar cells; protein plugs → ductal obstruction → calcification
  • Idiopathic: 2nd most common
  • Genetic/hereditary: PRSS1 (trypsinogen gene) mutation = hereditary pancreatitis; SPINK1, CFTR mutations
  • Cystic fibrosis (most common cause in children)
  • Autoimmune (IgG4), obstructive (stricture, tumour), hypercalcaemia
  • Path: Irreversible fibrosis, ductal stenosis and dilation, calcification, loss of exocrine and endocrine function; progressive destruction over years
💬 Clinical Features
  • Chronic abdominal pain: epigastric, radiating to back; often severe, continuous; worse with alcohol and fatty food; relieved by sitting forward; often leads to opioid dependence
  • Exocrine insufficiency: steatorrhoea (pale, greasy, offensive, floats), malabsorption, weight loss, fat-soluble vitamin deficiency (A, D, E, K) — usually when <10% pancreatic function remains
  • Endocrine insufficiency: diabetes mellitus (type 3c — brittle DM, prone to hypoglycaemia)
  • Jaundice (if CBD stricture)
  • Acute-on-chronic exacerbations
  • ↑Risk of pancreatic adenocarcinoma (~4% lifetime)
🔬 Investigations
  • Amylase/lipase may be normal or mildly elevated (burnt-out gland)
  • CT abdomen: calcifications in pancreatic duct (pathognomonic), ductal dilation, atrophy
  • MRCP: ductal anatomy, strictures, stones (no radiation)
  • Endoscopic USS (EUS): most sensitive; hyperechoic foci, dilated duct, lobularity
  • Faecal elastase-1: <200 μg/g = exocrine insufficiency (simple, non-invasive)
  • HbA1c, glucose (endocrine insufficiency); fat-soluble vitamin levels
  • CA19-9 if malignancy suspected
💊 Management
  • Lifestyle: Absolute alcohol abstinence (slows progression); smoking cessation; small low-fat meals
  • Pain management: WHO analgesic ladder; paracetamol → tramadol → opioids (careful — addiction risk); antioxidants (limited evidence); endoscopic therapy (stone removal, ductal stenting — ERCP); coeliac plexus block/neurolysis; surgery (Frey's, Beger's procedure or Whipple's if head disease)
  • Exocrine replacement: Pancreatic enzyme replacement therapy (PERT) — Creon® (pancrelipase) capsules with ALL meals and snacks; dose titrated; aim for <3 stools/day, no steatorrhoea; add PPI to optimise pH
  • Endocrine: insulin for type 3c DM; careful (no glucagon reserve → hypoglycaemia risk)
  • Fat-soluble vitamin supplementation (ADEK); dietary review
  • Annual surveillance for pancreatic cancer in hereditary pancreatitis
⚡ High Yield
  • Chronic pancreatitis → calcification on CT (pathognomonic); steatorrhoea + DM + chronic pain = classic triad
  • Faecal elastase-1 <200 μg/g = exocrine insufficiency → start Creon®
  • Type 3c DM (pancreatogenic): brittle, prone to hypoglycaemia, no glucagon reserve
  • Creon® (pancrelipase) in Australia — dose with ALL meals/snacks; needs acidic pH (add PPI)
  • Hereditary pancreatitis (PRSS1): ↑risk pancreatic cancer; annual EUS/MRCP surveillance

🔬 Pathophysiology & Aetiology
  • Obstruction of appendiceal lumen → ↑intraluminal pressure → vascular compromise → bacterial overgrowth → inflammation → necrosis/perforation
  • Causes: fecalith/faecal stone (most common in adults), lymphoid hyperplasia (most common in children — often post-viral), tumour, foreign body, parasites (Enterobius)
  • Peak incidence: 10–30 years; slight male predominance; lifetime risk ~7%
  • Organisms: E. coli, Bacteroides fragilis, Peptostreptococcus
💬 Classic Symptom Progression
  • 1. Periumbilical pain: diffuse, visceral (T8-10 afferents) — dull, colicky
  • 2. Anorexia, nausea, vomiting (after pain onset — nausea AFTER pain distinguishes from gastroenteritis)
  • 3. Migration to RLQ: parietal peritoneum involved → somatic, sharp, localised pain at McBurney's point (1/3 distance from RASIS to umbilicus)
  • 4. Fever (low-grade initially; high = perforation)
  • Atypical presentations: pelvic appendix (rectal/urinary symptoms), retrocaecal (flank pain), pregnancy (RUQ as uterus pushes appendix up)
🩺 Clinical Signs
  • McBurney's point tenderness: maximum tenderness at 1/3 from RASIS
  • Rovsing's sign: RLQ pain on palpation of LLQ (peritoneal irritation)
  • Psoas sign: RLQ pain on hip flexion against resistance (or passive extension) — retrocaecal appendix
  • Obturator sign: RLQ pain on internal rotation of flexed right hip — pelvic appendix
  • Rebound tenderness + guarding: peritoneal irritation
  • Dunphy's sign: ↑pain with coughing
🔬 Investigations
  • FBC: neutrophilic leukocytosis (WCC >10 × 10⁹/L)
  • CRP: elevated; ↑↑ suggests perforation/abscess
  • Urine MCS: exclude UTI; mild pyuria can occur with adjacent inflammation
  • Pregnancy test (females of reproductive age): exclude ectopic pregnancy
  • USS abdomen: first-line in children and pregnant women; appendix >6mm non-compressible = appendicitis; limited by operator and body habitus
  • CT abdomen/pelvis (with IV ± oral contrast): gold standard in adults; most sensitive/specific; shows periappendiceal fat stranding, appendix diameter >6mm, fecolith; exposes to radiation
  • MRI: preferred in pregnancy (no radiation)
  • Alvarado score (MANTRELS): clinical scoring tool (1 point each): Migration of pain to RLQ, Anorexia, Nausea/vomiting, Tenderness in RLQ, Rebound tenderness, Elevated temperature, Leukocytosis (2 pts), Shift to left. Score ≥7 = high probability; ≤4 = low probability
💊 Management
  • Surgical (gold standard): Laparoscopic appendectomy — lower wound infection, faster recovery; urgent for perforated/gangrenous appendicitis
  • Open appendectomy: if laparoscopic not available; in some perforated cases
  • Antibiotics: IV cefazolin (or ceftriaxone) + metronidazole perioperatively; broadened to pip-tazo if perforated
  • Non-operative management (NOM): increasingly accepted for uncomplicated appendicitis (no fecalith, no perforation); IV antibiotics (ceftriaxone + metronidazole) → oral; 20–30% will need surgery within 1 year; discussed with patient
  • Periappendiceal abscess: CT-guided drainage + IV antibiotics → interval appendicectomy in 6–8 weeks
  • Perforated appendicitis: urgent surgery; broad-spectrum antibiotics; washout
⚠️ Complications & Differentials
  • Perforation → peritonitis, abscess, sepsis; ↑risk with delay >24–36h, extremes of age
  • Wound infection; stump leak; adhesions/small bowel obstruction
  • Differentials: mesenteric adenitis (children), Meckel's diverticulitis, ovarian pathology (OC, torsion, ectopic), pelvic inflammatory disease (PID), Crohn's ileitis, caecal carcinoma, psoas abscess, urolithiasis
  • Carcinoid tumour: most common appendiceal tumour; usually tip; <2cm → appendicectomy curative; >2cm → right hemicolectomy
⚡ High Yield
  • Pain BEFORE nausea/vomiting (vs gastroenteritis: nausea first)
  • CT abdomen = gold standard in adults; USS first-line in children/pregnancy; MRI if pregnant
  • Alvarado ≥7 = high probability; ≤4 = low probability (observe/discharge)

🧬 Classification & Causes
  • Primary (spontaneous bacterial peritonitis — SBP): No intraabdominal source; cirrhosis + ascites (most common); nephrotic syndrome; peritoneal dialysis. Organisms: E. coli, Klebsiella, Streptococcus. Diagnosis: ascitic fluid ANC >250 cells/mm³
  • Secondary: Perforation of hollow viscus (DU, appendix, diverticulum, ischaemia), bowel obstruction with necrosis, post-surgical anastomotic leak, trauma, PID. Polymicrobial (gram-negative enteric bacteria + anaerobes)
  • Tertiary: Persistent peritonitis after adequate treatment of secondary; often resistant organisms; immunocompromised
💬 Clinical Features
  • Sudden/gradual-onset severe abdominal pain (worse on movement)
  • Board-like rigidity: involuntary guarding = peritoneal irritation
  • Rebound tenderness (Blumberg sign): pain on sudden release of pressure
  • Absent bowel sounds (ileus)
  • High fever, tachycardia, hypotension (septic shock)
  • Lying still (any movement worsens pain) — contrasts with renal/biliary colic (rolling around)
  • SBP may be subtle: increasing abdominal discomfort, encephalopathy, fever, deteriorating renal function
🔬 Investigations
  • FBC: neutrophilic leukocytosis (or leucopaenia in overwhelming sepsis)
  • CRP, lactate, blood cultures
  • UECr, LFTs, coagulation
  • Erect CXR: free air under diaphragm (pneumoperitoneum) = perforated viscus
  • CT abdomen/pelvis: identifies source, pneumoperitoneum, free fluid, abscess; essential for surgical planning
  • Diagnostic ascitic tap (SBP): >10mL; ANC, total protein, albumin, culture (inoculate blood culture bottles immediately)
💊 Management
  • Resuscitation (Sepsis 6): IV access × 2, aggressive IV fluids, O₂, blood cultures, IV antibiotics, catheter (hourly UO)
  • Secondary peritonitis — IV antibiotics: piperacillin-tazobactam (pip-tazo) + metronidazole (if anaerobic cover needed); or ceftriaxone + metronidazole
  • Emergency surgery: source control — laparotomy or laparoscopy; remove/repair source; peritoneal washout; drain placement
  • SBP management: IV ceftriaxone 2g daily × 5 days (or IV amoxicillin/clavulanate); IV albumin 1.5g/kg day 1, 1g/kg day 3 (reduces HRS risk and mortality); secondary prophylaxis: norfloxacin or ciprofloxacin PO indefinitely
  • ICU referral for septic shock
  • NGT decompression; urinary catheter; DVT prophylaxis
⚡ High Yield
  • SBP: cirrhosis + ascites + fever/abdominal pain/encephalopathy → diagnostic tap → ANC >250 cells/mm³ = SBP
  • SBP: ceftriaxone IV + albumin (day 1 and 3) → reduces hepatorenal syndrome mortality
  • Free air under diaphragm on erect CXR = perforated viscus → emergency surgery
  • Patient lies very still (movement worsens pain) vs renal colic (writhes/rolls)
  • Peritoneal dialysis peritonitis: cloudy effluent + ANC >100 → intraperitoneal antibiotics (vancomycin + gentamicin)

💊 Medical Management — Australian Context
  • Mild–moderate UC: mesalazine/5-ASA first-line; oral ± rectal depending on extent. Suppository for proctitis, enema for left-sided disease
  • UC maintenance: long-term mesalazine; immunomodulators or advanced therapy if steroid-dependent, relapsing or moderate–severe
  • Moderate–severe UC flare: oral steroids if outpatient; acute severe UC needs hospital care, IV steroids and early gastro/surgical input. Rescue: infliximab or ciclosporin
  • Crohn induction: corticosteroids for active disease; budesonide for mild ileocaecal disease. 5-ASA has limited benefit and is not core therapy
  • Moderate–severe Crohn: biologic/small-molecule therapy depending on phenotype, severity and PBS criteria; options include anti-TNF, vedolizumab, ustekinumab and newer agents
  • Perianal Crohn: MRI pelvis, EUA if needed, antibiotics for sepsis, drainage/seton for abscess/fistula, often anti-TNF therapy
  • Before immunosuppression/biologics: check infection risk, vaccination status, TB, hepatitis B/C, VZV immunity and baseline bloods
  • Faecal calprotectin: helps distinguish inflammatory from functional symptoms and monitor disease activity
🔬 Diagnosis & Monitoring
  • Colonoscopy + biopsy: key test for diagnosis, extent and histology
  • Stool tests: MCS/PCR and C. difficile testing during acute presentations or flares
  • Bloods: FBE, CRP/ESR, UEC, LFT, albumin, iron studies, B12/folate and vitamin D as relevant
  • MRI enterography: preferred for small bowel Crohn, strictures and fistulae; avoids radiation
  • CT abdomen/pelvis: useful for acute complications such as abscess, obstruction or perforation
  • Capsule endoscopy: for suspected small bowel Crohn only after stricture risk is considered
  • Drug monitoring: biologic trough and anti-drug antibody levels can guide dosing or switching
  • CRC surveillance: usually starts about 8 years after colitis symptom onset in extensive UC or Crohn colitis; interval depends on extent, inflammation, family history, dysplasia and PSC
🗺️ Approach to Diarrhoea
  • Acute diarrhoea: consider infection first, especially fever, travel, food exposure, outbreak, immunosuppression or recent antibiotics
  • Initial tests if concerning: stool MCS/PCR, C. difficile, FBE, CRP and UEC; prioritise hydration/supportive care
  • Bloody diarrhoea: consider IBD, infectious colitis, ischaemic colitis, diverticular bleeding, CRC and radiation proctitis
  • Chronic diarrhoea: consider IBD, IBS, coeliac disease, microscopic colitis, bile acid diarrhoea, malabsorption, medication/endocrine causes and CRC
  • Useful chronic tests: faecal calprotectin, coeliac serology, FBE, CRP, UEC, LFT, TFTs, iron studies; faecal elastase if steatorrhoea/pancreatic insufficiency suspected
  • Red flags: weight loss, iron-deficiency anaemia, persistent rectal bleeding, nocturnal symptoms, palpable mass, family history of bowel cancer, older age/new symptoms or major bowel habit change
  • STEC/EHEC concern: avoid empiric antibiotics and antimotility agents due to HUS risk
  • C. difficile: treat with guideline-directed therapy such as oral vancomycin or fidaxomicin rather than routine first-line metronidazole
⚡ High Yield
  • UC: colon-only, usually rectal onset, continuous mucosal inflammation, bloody diarrhoea, PSC association; colectomy can cure intestinal disease
  • Crohn: mouth-to-anus, skip lesions, transmural inflammation, strictures, fistulae, abscesses, perianal disease; surgery is not curative
  • Stones: UC (terminal ileum): bile salt malabsorption → gallstones; fat malabsorption → ↑oxalate absorption → calcium oxalate kidney stones; UC/ileostomy: low-volume and acidic urine → uric acid stones
  • Toxic megacolon: systemic toxicity + colonic dilatation; avoid antimotility agents/unnecessary colonoscopy; urgent medical and surgical input
  • 5-ASA: main role in UC; limited benefit in Crohn and not routine core therapy
  • Advanced therapy: biologics/small molecules for moderate–severe or refractory IBD depending on phenotype, severity and PBS criteria
  • PSC + IBD: suspect with cholestatic LFTs, pruritus, jaundice or cholangitis; increases CRC and cholangiocarcinoma risk
  • PSC correlation: 70–80% of PSC patients have UC but only a minority of UC patients develop PSC
  • Smoking: worsens Crohn and increases relapse/complications. It may appear protective in UC epidemiology, but should never be recommended
🧬 Epidemiology & Risk Factors
  • Major cause of cancer death in Australia; risk rises with age
  • Most cases are sporadic: adenoma–carcinoma sequence: APC → KRAS → TP53
  • Inherited syndromes: Lynch syndrome, FAP, MUTYH-associated polyposis, Peutz–Jeghers
  • Risk factors: age, adenomatous polyps, family history, IBD colitis, smoking, alcohol, obesity, type 2 diabetes, red/processed meat
  • Protective: exercise, healthy weight, fibre-rich diet, reduced alcohol and processed meat. Aspirin only for selected patients
💬 Clinical Presentation
  • Right-sided: occult bleeding, iron-deficiency anaemia, fatigue, weight loss, vague pain
  • Left-sided: change in bowel habit, visible bleeding, constipation/diarrhoea, obstruction
  • Rectal cancer: rectal bleeding, mucus, urgency, tenesmus, altered bowel habit
  • Red flags: IDA, persistent rectal bleeding, weight loss, abdominal/rectal mass, new bowel symptoms in older adults
  • DRE: important if rectal cancer or anorectal pathology is suspected
🔬 Screening & Diagnosis
  • NBCSP: FIT/iFOBT every 2 years for eligible Australians aged 45–74
  • Positive FIT → colonoscopy. FIT is a screening test, not diagnostic
  • Diagnosis: colonoscopy + biopsy
  • Staging: CT chest/abdomen/pelvis; MRI pelvis for rectal cancer
  • CEA: not diagnostic; useful for baseline and recurrence monitoring
  • High-risk patients: need individualised surveillance, not NBCSP alone
💊 Management
  • Colon cancer: surgical resection + lymphadenectomy; adjuvant chemotherapy for stage III and selected high-risk stage II
  • Rectal cancer: MRI-guided staging; total mesorectal excision ± neoadjuvant chemoradiotherapy
  • Metastatic disease: chemotherapy, targeted therapy or immunotherapy depending on RAS/BRAF/MSI/MMR status
  • Lynch/FAP: genetic counselling and specialist surveillance; FAP usually needs prophylactic colectomy

🧬 Pathophysiology
  • False diverticula: mucosa/submucosa herniate through weak points near vasa recta
  • Most common in sigmoid colon in Western populations
  • Risk factors: age, obesity, smoking, low activity, constipation, red meat, NSAIDs, steroids, opioids, immunosuppression
  • Diverticulosis: usually asymptomatic; incidental on CT/colonoscopy
  • Bleeding: painless haematochezia; often self-limiting but can be severe
💬 Diverticulitis Features
  • Acute left iliac fossa pain, fever, nausea, anorexia and altered bowel habit
  • Exam: local tenderness ± guarding; generalised peritonitis suggests perforation
  • Bloods often show leukocytosis and raised CRP
  • Pneumaturia, faecaluria or recurrent UTIs suggest colovesical fistula
  • Complicated: abscess, perforation, fistula, obstruction, sepsis or peritonitis
🔬 Investigations
  • CT abdomen/pelvis with IV contrast: preferred test if diagnosis is uncertain or complications suspected
  • CT: diverticula, bowel wall thickening, fat stranding, abscess, free air or obstruction
  • Bloods: FBE, CRP, UEC; urine testing if urinary symptoms
  • Avoid colonoscopy during acute attack
  • Consider colonoscopy after recovery if complicated disease, alarm symptoms, atypical imaging or no recent colonoscopy
💊 Management
  • Mild uncomplicated: outpatient care, fluids, analgesia, safety-netting
  • Antibiotics: selective, not automatic; use if systemic features, immunosuppression, frailty or complicated disease
  • Hospitalised/complicated: IV fluids, analgesia, IV antibiotics and surgical review if severe
  • Abscess: antibiotics ± image-guided drainage depending on size and stability
  • Perforation/peritonitis/sepsis: urgent surgical review
  • Elective surgery: individualised; not based only on number of episodes
  • Prevention: fibre-rich diet, exercise, weight optimisation, smoking cessation. No need to avoid nuts/seeds/popcorn
⚡ High Yield
  • Diverticulitis = left iliac fossa pain + fever + raised inflammatory markers
  • Uncomplicated diverticulitis does not always need antibiotics
  • Complicated = abscess, perforation, fistula, obstruction or sepsis
  • Colovesical fistula = recurrent UTIs + pneumaturia/faecaluria
  • Avoid colonoscopy during acute diverticulitis

🔬 Pathophysiology
  • Autoimmune T-cell mediated reaction to gliadin (component of gluten protein in wheat, rye, barley — not oats)
  • Gliadin → deamidated by tissue transglutaminase (tTG) → presented by HLA-DQ2 (90%) or HLA-DQ8 (5%) → CD4+ T-cell activation → villous atrophy
  • Autoantibodies: anti-tTG IgA (most sensitive/specific), anti-endomysial IgA (EMA), anti-deamidated gliadin peptide (anti-DGP)
  • Associated: HLA-DQ2/DQ8; Northern European descent; females > males; 1% prevalence in Australia
  • Strong associations: Type 1 DM (10%), Down syndrome, Turner syndrome, IgA deficiency (2–3%), other autoimmune diseases (thyroid, liver)
💬 Clinical Features
  • Classic (childhood): Diarrhoea/steatorrhoea, failure to thrive, abdominal distension, irritability, oedema
  • Non-classic (adult — more common): Often subtle; iron-deficiency anaemia (most common presentation in adults — malabsorption from proximal jejunum), fatigue, bloating, aphthous ulcers
  • Dermatitis herpetiformis: pruritic blistering rash on extensor surfaces (elbows, knees, buttocks) — gluten-sensitive; IgA deposits in skin
  • Osteoporosis (↓vitamin D + Ca²⁺ absorption)
  • Folate/B12 deficiency → megaloblastic anaemia
  • Elevated transaminases (hepatitis celiaçe)
  • Infertility, recurrent miscarriage
🔬 Investigations
  • Screen while ON gluten diet: Serum IgA anti-tTG (first-line); must check total serum IgA first (IgA deficiency → false negative)
  • If IgA deficient: anti-DGP IgG or anti-tTG IgG
  • Upper GI endoscopy + duodenal biopsy (D2 or D3): Confirm diagnosis; samples during gluten-containing diet
  • Histology — Marsh classification: 0=normal; 1=↑intraepithelial lymphocytes; 2=crypt hyperplasia; 3a=partial villous atrophy; 3b=subtotal; 3c=total villous atrophy; 4=hypoplasia
  • Diagnosis requires: positive serology + consistent histology + response to GFD (Cöliac Australasia criteria)
  • HLA-DQ2/DQ8 genotyping: high negative predictive value (if negative, coeliac excluded)
  • Bone density (DEXA): at diagnosis; FBC, ferritin, folate, B12, calcium, vitamin D
💊 Management
  • Strict lifelong gluten-free diet (GFD): Avoid wheat, rye, barley; oats controversial (most tolerate, but some sensitive). Rice, corn, potato, quinoa safe
  • Dietitian referral (essential); Coeliac Australia membership and support
  • Repeat serology at 3–6 months and 12 months (anti-tTG levels fall with GFD compliance)
  • Repeat duodenal biopsy in adults at 1–2 years to confirm mucosal healing (some recommend)
  • Supplementation: Iron, folate, calcium, vitamin D, B12 as deficient
  • Refractory coeliac disease (RCD): Persistent symptoms + villous atrophy despite strict GFD >12 months; exclude poor compliance first; budesonide/azathioprine; RCD type II → enteropathy-associated T-cell lymphoma (EATL)
  • Pneumococcal, meningococcal, Hib vaccines (↑infection risk with hyposplenism)
⚡ High Yield
  • IgA anti-tTG = best screening test; ALWAYS check total IgA first (2–3% IgA deficient → false negative)
  • Marsh 3c = total villous atrophy + crypt hyperplasia + ↑intraepithelial lymphocytes = classic histology
  • Dermatitis herpetiformis: extensor pruritic vesicles; IgA deposits in dermal papillae; responds to GFD + dapsone
  • Most common presentation in adults = iron-deficiency anaemia (occult GI blood loss not required!)
  • EATL (enteropathy-associated T-cell lymphoma) = most feared complication of uncontrolled/refractory coeliac
  • HLA-DQ2/DQ8 negative → coeliac excluded (100% NPV); useful if on GFD already
🔬 Pathophysiology & Epidemiology
  • Functional bowel disorder — no structural/biochemical abnormality; disorder of gut-brain interaction
  • Mechanisms: altered gut motility, visceral hypersensitivity (↓pain threshold), altered gut microbiome, intestinal permeability, psychosocial factors (anxiety, depression, somatisation)
  • Post-infectious IBS (PI-IBS): occurs after acute gastroenteritis (~10% of cases)
  • Prevalence: ~10–15% of Australians; female:male ~2:1; peak onset 20–40 years
  • Strong association with anxiety, depression, fibromyalgia, chronic fatigue, functional dyspepsia
🔬 Diagnosis — Rome IV Criteria
  • Recurrent abdominal pain ≥1 day/week for ≥3 months (last 3 months) associated with ≥2 of:
    1. Related to defaecation (better or worse)
    2. Associated with change in stool frequency
    3. Associated with change in stool form/consistency
  • Subtypes: IBS-C (constipation predominant), IBS-D (diarrhoea predominant), IBS-M (mixed), IBS-U (unclassified)
  • Investigations to exclude organic disease: FBC, CRP, coeliac serology (anti-tTG IgA), TFTs, faecal calprotectin (<50 μg/g argues against IBD); colonoscopy if >45 years, rectal bleeding, significant weight loss, family history CRC
  • Red flag features excluding IBS diagnosis: rectal bleeding, nocturnal symptoms, significant weight loss, age >50 onset, family history IBD/CRC, anaemia, ↑calprotectin, positive coeliac serology
💊 Management
  • Dietary: Low-FODMAP diet — developed at Monash University (Melbourne, Australia); eliminates fermentable oligosaccharides, disaccharides, monosaccharides and polyols; evidence base: ~70% response rate; supervised by dietitian; 3-phase protocol (elimination → reintroduction → personalisation)
  • Regular meals; ↑soluble fibre (psyllium); ↓insoluble fibre (may worsen IBS-D); avoid caffeine, alcohol, fatty foods, carbonated drinks; probiotics (modest benefit)
  • IBS-D pharmacological: antispasmodics (hyoscine butylbromide — Buscopan®, mebeverine); loperamide PRN; TCA (amitriptyline low-dose — ↓visceral sensitivity); rifaximin (non-absorbable antibiotic)
  • IBS-C pharmacological: Osmotic laxatives (polyethylene glycol — Movicol®); linaclotide (guanylate cyclase-C agonist); lubiprostone
  • Psychological: Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT), gut-directed hypnotherapy, mindfulness; SSRIs (amitriptyline or SSRIs for pain and psychological symptoms)
  • Explanation, reassurance, education; address psychosocial factors; long-term condition management
⚠️ Differentials to Exclude
  • Coeliac disease: anti-tTG IgA serology in ALL patients with IBS symptoms
  • IBD: faecal calprotectin >100–200 μg/g → further investigation; colonoscopy
  • Colorectal cancer: age >45, rectal bleeding, family history → colonoscopy
  • Small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO): glucose/lactulose hydrogen breath test
  • Bile acid malabsorption: SeHCAT test; ↑bile acids → IBS-D phenotype; responds to cholestyramine
  • Lactose/fructose intolerance: hydrogen breath test; elimination trial
  • Thyroid disease: TFTs (hyperthyroidism → diarrhoea; hypothyroidism → constipation)
  • Microscopic colitis (older females): colonoscopy with biopsy appears normal endoscopically but biopsy abnormal
⚡ High Yield
  • IBS = Rome IV criteria; diagnosis of exclusion; no structural abnormality on investigation
  • Low-FODMAP diet: developed at Monash University (Melbourne) — world-leading evidence for IBS; 70% response; must be supervised by dietitian
  • Faecal calprotectin <50 μg/g = argues strongly against IBD (NPV ~95%); elevated = colonoscopy needed
  • Always test anti-tTG IgA in suspected IBS — 5% have coeliac disease
  • Post-infectious IBS (PI-IBS): risk after bacterial gastroenteritis (especially Campylobacter)
  • No nocturnal symptoms, no rectal bleeding, no anaemia, no weight loss = supports IBS over organic disease
🔬 GI Stromal Tumours (GISTs)
  • Most common mesenchymal tumour of the GI tract; arise from interstitial cells of Cajal (pacemaker cells)
  • Location: Stomach (60%) > small intestine (30%) > colon/rectum
  • Mutations: KIT (c-kit) gain-of-function (80%) → ↑tyrosine kinase activity; PDGFRA (10%); wild-type GISTs (10%) — SDH deficiency, NF1, BRAF
  • Immunohistochemistry: CD117 (c-kit) positive (95%); CD34 positive; DOG1 positive
  • Malignant potential: based on size + mitotic rate (Miettinen-Lasota risk classification)
  • Presentation: Often asymptomatic/incidental; GI bleeding (intraluminal rupture), abdominal mass, pain, obstruction. Rarely metastasise to lymph nodes (unlike carcinomas)
  • Management: Surgical resection (primary treatment); neoadjuvant imatinib (Gleevec/Glivec) to reduce tumour size pre-operatively; adjuvant imatinib 400mg daily × 3 years for high-risk resected disease; metastatic: imatinib → sunitinib → regorafenib (if KIT/PDGFRA positive); avapritinib (PDGFRA D842V)
🔬 GI Neuroendocrine Tumours (GI-NETs / Carcinoids)
  • Well-differentiated neuroendocrine tumours arising from enterochromaffin cells; previously called "carcinoid" (term increasingly replaced by NET)
  • Most common sites: Appendix (40%), ileum/small bowel (30%), rectum (20%)
  • Carcinoid syndrome: Only occurs with liver metastases (bypasses portal circulation) → serotonin (5-HT) enters systemic circulation; features: flushing (niacin-coloured), episodic diarrhoea (watery), bronchoconstriction/wheezing, right-sided valvular heart disease (carcinoid heart — tricuspid regurgitation, pulmonary stenosis)
  • Markers: Chromogranin A (CgA) — most useful marker; 24h urine 5-HIAA (5-hydroxyindoleacetic acid — serotonin metabolite); plasma serotonin
  • Imaging: Ga-68 DOTATATE PET-CT (most sensitive — somatostatin receptor imaging); CT/MRI; EUS (pancreatic NETs)
  • Management: Localised: surgical resection (curative). Advanced/metastatic: octreotide LAR or lanreotide (somatostatin analogues — symptom control + anti-tumour); everolimus (mTOR inhibitor); sunitinib (pancreatic NET); peptide receptor radionuclide therapy (PRRT) with 177Lu-DOTATATE (PBS-listed in Australia for progressive midgut NETs — NETTER-1 trial); liver-directed therapy (TACE, ablation)
  • Appendiceal NETs <2cm → appendicectomy curative; >2cm → right hemicolectomy
  • Pellagra-like skin changes (niacin deficiency) in carcinoid syndrome (tryptophan used for serotonin synthesis)
⚡ High Yield
  • GIST: KIT/c-kit mutation → CD117+; stomach most common location; imatinib (Gleevec/Glivec) tyrosine kinase inhibitor
  • Carcinoid syndrome ONLY with liver metastases (hepatic metabolism normally inactivates serotonin)
  • Carcinoid triad: flushing + diarrhoea + right-sided valvular disease (RV/tricuspid). Note: wheezing also occurs
  • Pellagra (niacin deficiency) in carcinoid syndrome — tryptophan diverted to serotonin synthesis
  • Ga-68 DOTATATE PET-CT = gold standard imaging for NET staging (somatostatin receptor positive)
  • PRRT (177Lu-DOTATATE) = PBS-listed in Australia for progressive midgut NETs; evidence from NETTER-1 trial
🧬 Pathophysiology & Aetiology
  • Acquired vascular ectasias (dilated, tortuous mucosal and submucosal vessels); thin-walled with no muscular layer → fragile, bleeds easily
  • Degeneration of vascular walls (chronic intermittent low-grade obstruction of submucosal veins → arteriovenous communications)
  • Most common in RIGHT colon (caecum and ascending) despite left-sided predisposition for diverticular disease
  • Prevalence increases with age (>60 years); associated with: end-stage renal disease (ESRD), aortic stenosis (Heyde syndrome), von Willebrand disease (especially type 2A — acquired vWD with AS)
  • Heyde syndrome: Aortic stenosis + angiodysplasia + GI bleeding + acquired von Willebrand disease type 2A (high shear stress through stenotic aortic valve cleaves large vWF multimers); corrected with AVR
  • Also associated with: hereditary haemorrhagic telangiectasia (HHT/Osler-Weber-Rendu), cirrhosis, radiation, connective tissue disease
💬 Clinical Features & Management
  • Bleeding: Usually PAINLESS haematochezia (bright red or maroon); may be occult (iron-deficiency anaemia with no visible blood); recurrent episodes; typically self-limiting but can be massive
  • Elderly patient with recurrent unexplained lower GI bleeding or iron-deficiency anaemia
  • Investigations: Colonoscopy (gold standard — identifies and treats); CT angiography (active bleeding >0.5mL/min); tagged red cell scan (bleeding 0.1–0.5mL/min — slower, intermittent); capsule endoscopy (small bowel lesions); push enteroscopy/device-assisted enteroscopy
  • Endoscopic appearance: cherry-red, flat, vascular lesion; spider-like pattern; blanches with pressure
  • Endoscopic treatment: Argon plasma coagulation (APC) — first-line; band ligation; haemoclips
  • Pharmacological: Octreotide (somatostatin analogue — inhibits vasodilators, reduces splanchnic blood flow); oestrogen-progesterone therapy (modestly effective); thalidomide (severe refractory — inhibits angiogenesis); bevacizumab (anti-VEGF — in HHT)
  • Interventional radiology: Super-selective angiographic embolisation (active bleeding, failed endoscopy)
  • Surgery: resection for severe uncontrolled bleeding refractory to endoscopic/radiological management (right hemicolectomy if right-sided)
  • Heyde syndrome: Aortic valve replacement (AVR) resolves acquired vWD and ↓GI bleeding from angiodysplasia
⚡ High Yield
  • Angiodysplasia: painless haematochezia in elderly patient; right colon predominance; diagnosis by colonoscopy
  • Heyde syndrome: aortic stenosis + angiodysplasia + GI bleeding + acquired vWD type 2A — AVR resolves all
  • APC (argon plasma coagulation) = endoscopic treatment of choice
  • Distinguish from diverticular bleeding: both painless haematochezia in elderly; diverticular → usually left-sided (despite right-sided most likely to bleed), stops spontaneously in 80%; angiodysplasia → right-sided, recurrent, iron-deficiency anaemia
  • HHT (Osler-Weber-Rendu): autosomal dominant; telangiectasias on lips/tongue/fingertips; epistaxis; GI bleeding; AV malformations (lung, brain, liver)
